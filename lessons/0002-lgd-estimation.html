LGD Estimation: The Bimodal Problem

Why loss severity is harder to model than default probability — and how ML navigates a uniquely awkward distribution.

1. What LGD is and how it connects to capital

Recall the expected loss formula from Lesson 1: EL = PD × LGD × EAD. LGD is the fraction of the exposure the bank actually loses, conditional on default occurring.

Formally: 

LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate

Recovery Rate = Σ (discounted recovery cash flows) / EAD at default

The discounting matters: a partial repayment received three years into a workout is worth less than one received immediately. Basel requires recoveries to be discounted at the bank's effective cost of collecting the debt — typically using a hurdle rate of around 15% for workout cases.

LGD valueInterpretationTypical scenario
0%Full recovery — no lossWell-collateralised mortgage; borrower cures and repays
45%Basel regulatory floor for unsecured senior corporatesUnsecured corporate with partial recovery via liquidation
75%Severe lossJunior/subordinated debt, distressed assets
100%Total lossUnsecured consumer credit in personal bankruptcy with no assets
Why LGD matters more than it looks
In retail portfolios, PD gets most of the modelling attention — but LGD variation can be enormous. A bank with a large unsecured personal loans book might see LGD range from 40% to 90% across borrower segments. Getting LGD wrong by 20pp on a £2bn portfolio = £400m of miscalculated RWA.

2. The bimodal distribution: why standard regression breaks

LGD has a bounded range of [0, 1]. Unlike PD, which is estimated but often close to a symmetric distribution in log-odds space, observed LGDs cluster heavily at the extremes:

Stylised LGD distribution for a typical retail unsecured portfolio — mass at 0 (cures) and near 1 (total losses), thin middle.

This happens because defaulted accounts tend to resolve in one of two ways:

  • Cure: The borrower catches up on arrears and the account is brought back to performing. LGD ≈ 0 (the bank collects full cost of funds via interest). Around 20–40% of retail defaults cure in the first year.
  • Workout/loss: The account enters formal recovery. In unsecured consumer credit with limited assets, losses are frequently 60–100%.

The practical consequence: a linear regression trained on this distribution predicts values in the middle of the range (e.g., 0.45–0.55) where very few actual observations land. The model minimises squared error globally but is wrong for almost every individual case.

3. ML approaches that handle bimodality

Approach 1: Two-stage model (industry standard)

The most widely adopted ML approach decomposes the problem into two sub-models with different targets:

All defaults Defaulted accounts with completed recovery
→
Stage 1: Classifier P(cure) vs P(loss)
XGBoost / RF
→
Stage 2: Regressor Severity | loss occurred
XGBoost / RF / Beta reg
→
Final LGD P(loss) × severity
+ 0 × P(cure)

Stage 1 handles the bimodality by treating the cure/loss split as a classification problem. Stage 2 estimates loss severity only within the loss population, where the distribution is less extreme. The outputs are combined: 

LGD = P(loss|default) × E[severity | loss occurred]
    = (1 − P(cure)) × severity_estimate
Model risk implication
A two-stage model has two error sources. Validation must evaluate Stage 1 and Stage 2 independently before assessing the combined output. A model that predicts cure probability well but severity poorly will produce a plausible Gini on the combined output while systematically mispricing individual segments.

Approach 2: Beta regression

Beta regression uses the Beta distribution as its error structure. The Beta is defined on (0, 1), has flexible shape (U-shaped, J-shaped, or symmetric), and directly models proportions. It avoids the boundary prediction problem of linear regression and is interpretable.

The limitation: it cannot assign mass exactly at 0 or 1 — but observed LGDs are often exactly 0 (cure) or exactly 1 (total write-off). A zero-one inflated Beta regression adds point masses at the boundaries, but becomes complex to validate.

Approach 3: XGBoost / Random Forest (direct)

Tree-based models can approximate bimodal distributions via deep leaf splits, but they do this indirectly. With sufficient depth and data, they tend to perform well on discrimination (Gini, MAE), but their predictions will still be pulled toward the centre compared to the two-stage approach. In practice, many banks use XGBoost as the Stage 2 regressor inside a two-stage architecture.

4. What drives LGD: key feature categories

LGD is not primarily about the borrower's creditworthiness (that drives PD). It is about how much can be recovered — which depends on collateral, deal structure, and the resolution process.

Feature categoryExamplesMechanism
Collateral Loan-to-Value (LTV), collateral type (property, equipment, unsecured), collateral age Higher quality / lower LTV collateral → higher recovery → lower LGD. Most important single driver for secured lending.
Seniority & structure Senior secured > senior unsecured > mezzanine > equity. Covenant package. Senior secured creditors are paid first in liquidation. Seniority cliff effect: crossing from senior unsecured to subordinated can double LGD.
Borrower characteristics Industry sector, company size, jurisdiction, business model (asset-heavy vs asset-light) Asset-heavy industries (real estate, manufacturing) produce higher recoveries than service/tech firms with few tangible assets.
Resolution process Time in workout, resolution method (sale, restructuring, write-off), jurisdiction's insolvency regime UK administration vs US Chapter 11 vs German Insolvenzordnung produce systematically different LGDs. Time to resolution is inversely correlated with recovery — longer = more costs eroding recovery.
Macro / systemic Property price index, unemployment rate, credit market conditions, industry stress index Recoveries are deeply cyclical. In downturns, collateral values fall simultaneously with defaults, amplifying losses. This is the core of the downturn LGD requirement.

5. The downturn LGD requirement

Basel mandates that the LGD used for capital calculation reflects losses during a period of economic stress — not the long-run average. This is the downturn LGD concept. The intuition: defaults and low recoveries co-occur during recessions, so using average LGD systematically underestimates losses when the bank most needs capital.

The correlation problem
If PD rises in a recession AND LGD rises simultaneously (because collateral values fall), then EL = PD × LGD is not additive of two independent risks — it is amplified. The 2008 crisis made this vivid: US mortgage LGDs that averaged 20% in benign periods reached 60%+ in 2009–2011 as house prices fell 30–40%. Any model trained only on 2002–2007 data was catastrophically wrong.

The standard approach to downturn LGD:

  1. Identify the reference period — typically the period in your observed data with highest default rates or lowest recovery rates (e.g., 2008–2010 for most developed markets)
  2. Calculate conditional LGD — estimate LGD using only data from the reference period, or condition on macro variables that define the downturn
  3. Apply add-on or override — if the ML model produces a through-the-cycle (TTC) LGD, apply a regulatory add-on or a conservative scalar to reach the downturn floor
  4. Document the adjustment — the methodology for the downturn add-on is itself model risk governed and must be independently validated
ML-specific challenge
ML models trained on rich feature sets may learn macro conditions implicitly (e.g., "LTV=80% in 2009 → high LGD"), making the downturn conditioning harder to isolate. Validators must verify that the model does not inadvertently produce low LGD estimates for stressed scenarios where macro features were not observed in training. Partial Dependence Plots (PDPs) on macro features are the standard diagnostic.

6. The data challenges unique to LGD

LGD modelling has data problems that PD modelling does not:

  • Sparsity: Defaults are rare by design (good lending!). A bank with a 1% default rate on 100,000 accounts has 1,000 defaults per year. Of those, recoveries from large accounts can take 3–7 years to complete. A 10-year data window might produce only 5,000–8,000 completed recoveries — too few for a complex ML model on a segmented portfolio.
  • Censoring: Accounts still in recovery at the model development date are right-censored — you know the recovery so far, but not the final LGD. Including these inflates the dataset but introduces bias. Excluding them creates survivorship bias (slow-resolving cases with low recoveries are under-represented). Survival models or Tobit regression are sometimes used to handle this.
  • Vintage effects: Lending standards, product mix, and economic conditions change over time. Data from 10 years ago may represent a fundamentally different portfolio. ML models must be validated for concept drift across vintages.

7. How LGD models are evaluated

MetricWhat it measuresLGD-specific note
MAE / RMSE Mean Absolute / Root Mean Squared Error against realised LGDs RMSE penalises large errors more — important because large misses on high-LGD accounts drive capital errors
Gini / AUC Discrimination between high- and low-LGD accounts Lower Ginis than PD models are typical (40–60%) — LGD is inherently noisier
Bias test Mean predicted LGD vs mean realised LGD, in aggregate and by segment Regulators focus on segment-level bias, not just portfolio average. A model unbiased in aggregate can still systematically underestimate a risky segment.
Downturn test Does the model produce LGD estimates consistent with observed downturn periods? Often done via scenario analysis: apply 2008–2010 macro inputs and verify predictions are credible
Retrieval Practice

1. A mortgage defaults with EAD = £200,000. The bank recovers £140,000 after discounting. What is the LGD?

Correct. LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate = 1 − (140,000 / 200,000) = 1 − 0.70 = 0.30 = 30%.
LGD = 1 − (recovered / EAD) = 1 − (140,000 / 200,000) = 1 − 0.70 = 30%. The bank loses 30% of the exposure.

2. Why does a standard linear regression model perform poorly as a direct LGD estimator?

Correct. The bimodal distribution means most observations are near 0 (cures) or near 1 (total losses). A linear model minimises squared error globally and ends up predicting the middle — wrong for nearly every individual account.
The problem is distributional. LGDs cluster at extremes (0 = cure, near 1 = total loss). A linear model minimises global MSE and therefore predicts the low-density middle range. It is wrong for almost every individual prediction even though its average error is small.

3. In a two-stage LGD model, what does Stage 1 predict?

Correct. Stage 1 is a binary classifier that separates cures from losses. It handles the bimodality by routing accounts before severity estimation. Stage 2 then predicts severity only within the loss population.
Stage 1 handles the bimodality by classifying accounts as cure vs workout. Only Stage 2 predicts the magnitude of loss — and only for accounts classified as workout. Combining the two: LGD = P(loss) × E[severity | loss].

4. Basel requires "downturn LGD" for capital calculation. What is the core reason?

Correct. PD-LGD correlation is the key insight. When defaults spike in a recession, recoveries fall simultaneously (collateral values drop, buyers scarce). Using average LGD ignores this amplifying effect — capital would be systematically too low exactly when losses are highest.
The issue is PD-LGD correlation. In a recession, both PD and LGD rise together — collateral values fall just as more borrowers default. Average LGD from benign periods underestimates this co-movement. Basel's downturn LGD captures losses under the same stressed conditions that produce elevated PDs.

5. A bank has 10 years of default data but many recovery processes are still open at the model development date. What statistical problem does this create?

Correct. Right-censoring: you observe partial recovery data for in-progress cases but not the final LGD. Excluding them creates selection bias (slow-resolving, higher-LGD cases are dropped). Including them naively introduces downward bias. Survival analysis or Tobit regression are the standard treatments.
This is right-censoring. For accounts still in recovery, you only know the LGDs collected so far — not the final outcome. Excluding these cases biases the sample toward faster-resolving (often lower-LGD) cases. Survival models or Tobit regression explicitly model the censoring rather than ignoring it.
