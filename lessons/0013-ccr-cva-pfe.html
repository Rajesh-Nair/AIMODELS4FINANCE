Counterparty Credit Risk: CVA & PFE

Why derivative exposure is fundamentally different from loan exposure — and how ML makes the simulation cheaper and the pricing more accurate.

1. What makes counterparty credit risk different

In a loan, the exposure is known: the bank lends £1m and the worst case is losing £1m. In a derivative contract (an interest rate swap, FX forward, credit default swap), the exposure depends on future market movements that neither party controls:

  • If rates move in your favour, the counterparty owes you money — you have credit exposure
  • If rates move against you, you owe them money — your exposure is zero (you would pay, not receive)
  • This exposure can fluctuate from zero to very large over the life of the contract

Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) is the risk that a counterparty defaults at a time when the derivative has positive value to the bank. It requires modelling not just the counterparty's creditworthiness (as in loan PD), but also the joint distribution of market variables and default events — a fundamentally harder problem.

2. Potential Future Exposure (PFE)

PFE measures the worst-case exposure at a given confidence level (typically 95% or 97.5%) over time. Unlike a loan's fixed exposure, PFE is a path — it shows how credit exposure could evolve over the contract's life:

Stylised exposure profile for an interest rate swap. EE = Expected Exposure (average across scenarios); PFE = 95th percentile. Both are zero at maturity (contract ends). The hump shape reflects increasing uncertainty early, then amortisation as the trade approaches expiry.

PFE is computed via Monte Carlo simulation: generate thousands of future market scenarios (interest rate paths, FX paths, credit spread paths), compute the mark-to-market value of the derivative in each scenario and at each future date, take the 95th percentile across scenarios at each date. This is computationally intensive — a large dealer bank may have millions of derivative trades, each requiring thousands of simulation paths.

3. CVA — the price of counterparty risk

CVA (Credit Valuation Adjustment) is the market value of counterparty credit risk: how much should the bank discount the derivative's fair value to account for the possibility that the counterparty defaults before maturity? 

CVA ≈ −LGD × Σₜ EE(t) × PD(t, t+Δt) × DF(t)

where:
  EE(t)         = Expected Exposure at time t
  PD(t, t+Δt)  = Marginal probability of default in [t, t+Δt]
  DF(t)         = Discount factor to today
  LGD           = Loss Given Default (typically 40–60% for derivatives)

CVA is marked to market daily — it moves as credit spreads, interest rates, and FX rates move. When a counterparty's CDS spread widens (creditworthiness deteriorates), CVA increases (the derivative becomes worth less to the bank). This daily P&L volatility is captured in the CVA desk — a specialised trading desk that hedges CVA.

4. The XVA family

CVA — Credit Valuation Adjustment

Cost of counterparty default risk to the bank. Reduces derivative fair value. The bank's cost of the counterparty's credit risk.

DVA — Debit Valuation Adjustment

Mirror of CVA: the counterparty's cost of the bank's credit risk. Increases derivative value when the bank's own credit deteriorates. Controversial — recognising a gain when your own credit worsens.

FVA — Funding Valuation Adjustment

Cost of funding uncollateralised derivatives. When a derivative is not collateralised (no CSA), the bank must fund the exposure from its balance sheet — FVA captures this funding cost.

MVA — Margin Valuation Adjustment

Cost of posting initial margin under SIMM (Standard Initial Margin Model, mandatory for bilateral derivatives under IM regulation). The cost of tying up collateral at CCPs or bilateral counterparties.
KVA — Capital Valuation Adjustment
A fifth adjustment — KVA (Capital VA) — captures the cost of holding regulatory capital against the derivative over its lifetime. Less universally adopted but important for full trade economics. The total XVA package (CVA + DVA + FVA + MVA + KVA) is subtracted from the "risk-free" derivative fair value to get the fully loaded price.

5. Where ML enters CCR

Neural network exposure approximation (surrogate models)

The central computational bottleneck in CCR is the Monte Carlo simulation for PFE and CVA. Running full repricing simulations for millions of trades across thousands of scenarios is extremely expensive — particularly for the real-time CVA sensitivities needed by the CVA desk for hedging.

Neural network surrogate models (also called metamodels or emulators) learn the mapping from market risk factors to derivative value across the simulation scenarios, replacing the full pricer with a fast approximation: 

Full pricer: V = f(rates, spreads, FX, vol, ...) → expensive, 0.1–100ms per call
NN surrogate: V̂ = g_θ(rates, spreads, FX, vol, ...) → ~1μs per call

Accuracy: |V − V̂| < ε for well-trained surrogate on in-distribution scenarios

The speedup enables real-time CVA sensitivities (Greeks), scenario-based XVA, and intraday PFE updates that would be computationally infeasible with full repricing.

Wrong-Way Risk (WWR) detection

Wrong-Way Risk occurs when the counterparty's default probability is positively correlated with the derivative's exposure: the counterparty is most likely to default exactly when the trade is most in-the-money to the bank. This amplifies CVA beyond what independence-assuming models would predict.

ML models — particularly tree-based models and neural networks — can capture the non-linear correlation structure between market risk factors and counterparty credit quality that linear WWR models miss.

Initial Margin (SIMM) and MVA optimisation

Under bilateral margin regulation, initial margin is computed using ISDA's SIMM model. ML is used to optimise trade structures that minimise IM requirements — given the high cost of MVA, traders actively seek IM-efficient hedges. Reinforcement learning for portfolio compression (finding trades that reduce IM at minimal economic impact) is an active research area at large dealer banks.

6. FRTB-CVA: the new regulatory capital framework

The FRTB-CVA framework (part of Basel IV / FRTB, finalisaed 2019) significantly changed how banks must capitalise CVA risk. Three approaches:

ApproachMethodWho uses it
BA-CVA (Basic) Supervisory formula using notional × credit spread × duration. Simple but high capital. Smaller banks, limited derivatives books
SA-CVA (Standard) Sensitivity-based: compute CVA sensitivities to market risk factors, apply regulatory risk weights. Requires full CVA delta, vega, curvature computation. Mid-tier and large banks with the systems to compute sensitivities
IMA-CVA (Internal) Full internal model approval — own ES model for CVA P&L. Highest complexity, lowest capital (potentially). Rarely approved by regulators. Largest global dealer banks only
Model risk in CCR
CVA models require simultaneous market risk modelling (for exposure) and credit risk modelling (for PD and LGD). Model validation teams need cross-disciplinary expertise rarely found in a single team. The interaction between wrong-way risk, correlation assumptions, and netting set structure makes CCR one of the most complex validation challenges in banking. SR 11-7 applies, and CCR models are invariably High-tier.
Retrieval Practice

1. A bank has an interest rate swap with a counterparty where the bank receives fixed / pays floating. Rates fall sharply. What happens to the bank's CVA?

Correct. When rates fall, the present value of the fixed receipts the bank is due exceeds the floating payments — the swap is in-the-money (positive MTM for the bank). A counterparty defaulting now would cost the bank more. CVA rises because the expected exposure (EE) has increased. Both market moves and credit spread changes affect CVA daily.
CVA = cost to the bank of counterparty default. When rates fall, the receive-fixed swap becomes in-the-money — the bank is owed more. A counterparty default now causes a larger loss. Higher EE → higher CVA. CVA moves with both market risk factors (affecting exposure) and credit spreads (affecting default probability). The CVA desk hedges both dimensions.

2. What is Wrong-Way Risk (WWR) in CCR, and why does it amplify CVA beyond standard model assumptions?

Correct. Standard CVA assumes independence between exposure and default probability. WWR arises when they are correlated: the market move that makes the trade valuable to the bank is the same move that pushes the counterparty toward default (e.g., a commodity producer selling commodity forwards — if commodity prices fall, the bank has high exposure AND the counterparty is financially stressed). CVA under WWR can be multiples of the independence assumption.
WWR is a correlation problem. The independence assumption in the standard CVA formula (EE computed separately from PD) breaks down when the market factor driving exposure is correlated with the counterparty's credit quality. Classic example: a mining company selling gold forwards to the bank — when gold prices fall, the bank's exposure (receiving gold price) is high AND the mining company is financially stressed. This positive correlation amplifies CVA significantly.

3. Why are neural network surrogate models valuable for CVA computation?

Correct. Full derivative repricing per scenario can take milliseconds to seconds per trade. At millions of trades × thousands of scenarios × multiple time steps, this is computationally prohibitive for real-time use. A trained NN surrogate approximates the same mapping in microseconds — 1,000–100,000× faster — enabling real-time Greeks computation, intraday scenario analysis, and live XVA pricing on a trading desk.
The surrogate model's value is speed. Full Monte Carlo repricing per derivative is 0.1–100ms; a trained neural network achieves ~1μs for the same mapping. This 1,000× speedup transforms what was a nightly batch calculation into a real-time tool: CVA Greeks for live hedging, intraday PFE for risk monitoring, instant XVA pricing for new trade evaluation. The model doesn't eliminate simulation — it compresses it.

4. FVA (Funding Valuation Adjustment) applies specifically to which type of derivative?

Correct. FVA captures the funding cost of positions that are not collateralised. When a derivative has no CSA (Credit Support Annex requiring margin posting), the bank must fund any in-the-money position from its own balance sheet. Collateralised trades hedge this funding risk naturally — daily margin exchange means exposure is almost always near zero. FVA is specifically the cost of the funding mismatch on uncollateralised trades.
FVA arises from funding mismatch on uncollateralised trades. Under a CSA, margin is posted daily — exposure stays near zero, no funding is needed. Without a CSA, the bank funds the in-the-money position from its balance sheet at its own cost of funds. FVA charges the trade for this funding cost. Post-2008 mandatory clearing rules pushed many standard derivatives to CCPs (eliminating FVA), but bespoke/exotic OTC trades remain uncollateralised and carry FVA.
