Fraud Detection: Recall Above All

Why the standard ML metrics mislead in fraud — and how supervised models and autoencoders complement each other.

1. The defining constraint: asymmetric costs

Fraud detection is not a standard classification problem. The classes are massively imbalanced (fraud rates in card transactions typically run 0.1–1%) and the cost of errors is radically asymmetric:

Predicted: Fraud
Predicted: Legit
Actual: Fraud
True Positive Low cost Fraud caught; small friction to customer
False Negative Very high cost Fraud missed: full transaction loss + regulatory penalty + reputational damage
Actual: Legit
False Positive Moderate cost Legitimate customer blocked: friction, call-centre cost, churn risk
True Negative Zero cost Correct approval; no action needed

The consequence: Recall (sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)) is the primary metric. Missing fraud is far more costly than blocking a legitimate transaction. But recall alone is not enough — a model that flags every transaction as fraud achieves 100% recall and is useless. The operating constraint is the False Positive Rate: compliance teams and fraud operations have finite capacity to investigate alerts.

The precision-recall operating point
Banks set a decision threshold that trades off recall against precision (and thus alert volume). A threshold producing 90% recall and 5% precision means for every 20 alerts investigated, 1 is real fraud — workable for high-value transactions. For low-value card transactions, that ratio may be unsustainable. The threshold is a business decision as much as a technical one.

2. Why accuracy and AUC-ROC mislead here

On a dataset that is 0.5% fraud, a model that predicts "legitimate" for every transaction achieves 99.5% accuracy. The number is meaningless.

AUC-ROC is better, but also misleading at extreme imbalance: it evaluates across all thresholds including very high false-positive-rate regions that are operationally impossible. The Precision-Recall (PR) curve is the correct visualisation — it focuses on the region that matters: high recall with acceptable precision.

Stylised PR curve. The operating point (threshold choice) moves along this curve — right means more recall, lower precision. Area under PR curve (AUPRC) is the preferred single-number metric for imbalanced fraud problems.

3. Supervised learning: learning from labelled fraud

The primary approach at most banks: train a gradient boosted model (XGBoost, LightGBM) on a labelled dataset of historical transactions, where each transaction is tagged as confirmed fraud or confirmed legitimate.

Handling class imbalance

With 0.5% fraud, naively trained models learn "always predict legitimate." Four standard techniques:

  • Class weighting: assign a higher loss penalty to fraud misclassification. XGBoost's scale_pos_weight parameter implements this directly. Simplest and most robust approach.
  • SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling): generate synthetic fraud examples by interpolating between real fraud cases in feature space. Increases fraud representation in training. Risk: synthetic examples may not reflect real fraud patterns.
  • Undersampling: randomly remove majority (legitimate) examples to balance the dataset. Fast; may discard useful information.
  • Threshold calibration: train on imbalanced data but lower the decision threshold — classify as fraud at score > 0.1 rather than > 0.5. Operationally equivalent to class weighting but applied post-training.

4. Autoencoders: detecting novel fraud

Supervised models have a structural limitation: they only detect fraud patterns they have seen before. A novel fraud attack — a new technique, a compromised merchant network, a synthetic identity scheme — will produce transactions that look unlike anything in the training set. The supervised model assigns low fraud probability because it has no template for this pattern.

Autoencoders solve this with a fundamentally different approach: learn what normal looks like, then flag anything that doesn't fit.

Training: only on legitimate transactions. The encoder-decoder learns a compressed representation of normal transaction patterns. When a fraudulent transaction — which has a different statistical structure — is passed through the network, the decoder cannot reconstruct it accurately. The reconstruction error is high, triggering an alert.

Supervised + autoencoder: the hybrid
Best-practice fraud systems combine both approaches. The supervised model catches known fraud patterns with high precision. The autoencoder catches novel patterns the supervised model misses. The outputs are combined — either by ensemble scoring or by routing to different investigation queues (known fraud vs anomaly) — with different threshold policies for each.

5. Adversarial drift: the fastest model decay in finance

Fraud detection models decay faster than almost any other model in banking, because fraudsters actively adapt. Unlike a credit customer whose behaviour changes gradually with macroeconomic conditions, a fraud ring learns which transaction patterns trigger the model and deliberately stays below the threshold. This is adversarial drift.

Consequences for model governance:

  • Monthly or weekly monitoring of recall and alert-to-fraud conversion rate — much more frequent than credit models (quarterly)
  • Champion-challenger architecture running continuously: the current model (champion) vs. a retrained version (challenger), with live traffic split between them
  • Rapid retraining pipelines: fraud models are often retrained weekly or biweekly using recent confirmed fraud labels — more like a software deployment pipeline than a traditional model lifecycle
  • Concept drift detection: statistical tests (PSI, KS test on score distribution) running in real-time to detect when the fraud population has shifted beyond what the model was trained on
SR 11-7 in real-time
The rapid retraining cycle for fraud models compresses the SR 11-7 model lifecycle dramatically. A model "change" that would take months to validate in a credit risk context — new features, updated hyperparameters — may need to be deployed in days in a fraud context. Banks must develop expedited validation processes for fraud models that maintain governance rigour without the full validation cycle. This is an active area of model risk innovation.
Retrieval Practice

1. A fraud model achieves 99.6% accuracy on a dataset where 0.4% of transactions are fraudulent. Why is this number misleading?

Correct. When fraud is 0.4% of transactions, the trivial model — never predict fraud — achieves 99.6% accuracy. This model catches zero frauds. Accuracy is meaningless for imbalanced classification. Use Recall, Precision, and Area Under the PR Curve instead.
The trivial classifier ("always predict legitimate") achieves 99.6% accuracy on this dataset by catching zero fraud. Accuracy is dominated by the majority class and tells you nothing about performance on the minority class that matters. Precision-Recall metrics and AUPRC are the correct measures for highly imbalanced fraud problems.

2. An autoencoder for fraud detection is trained only on legitimate transactions. Why does this make it useful for novel fraud?

button class="option" data-correct="false">It generates synthetic fraud examples that supplement the supervised model's training set
Correct. The autoencoder learns the manifold of normal transactions. Anything that deviates from that manifold — including novel fraud types the model has never been trained to recognise — produces high reconstruction error. This is the key advantage over supervised models, which are blind to fraud patterns they haven't seen in labelled training data.
The autoencoder does not learn what fraud looks like — it learns what normal looks like. When a novel fraud transaction (different statistical structure from normal) passes through the encoder-decoder, the decoder reconstructs it poorly: high ‖x − x̂‖. This flags it as an anomaly. Supervised models miss novel fraud because they have no training label for it; the autoencoder catches it because it deviates from normal, regardless of whether it's a known fraud type.

3. A fraud model's recall drops from 88% to 71% over three months with no change to the model. What is the most likely explanation?

Correct. Adversarial drift is the dominant cause of rapid recall degradation in fraud models. Fraudsters probe the model's responses, identify which transaction characteristics trigger alerts, and modify their attack patterns to fall below the detection threshold. This is why fraud models require far more frequent retraining and monitoring than credit or market risk models.
Recall decay over months with no model changes is the signature of adversarial drift. Fraud rings test and learn which patterns trigger the model, then adapt. The model's features no longer discriminate the new fraud pattern. The response is rapid retraining with recent labelled fraud, potentially combined with autoencoder anomaly scoring to catch the new pattern before sufficient labels are accumulated.

4. XGBoost's scale_pos_weight parameter is set to 200 on a fraud dataset. What does this do, and why?

Correct. scale_pos_weight is a class weighting parameter. Setting it to 200 (≈ ratio of negatives to positives) makes each false negative in the fraud class 200× more costly in the gradient calculation. The model learns to prioritise recall over precision — matching the real-world cost asymmetry where missed fraud is far more costly than false alarms.
scale_pos_weight multiplies the loss gradient for the positive (fraud) class. At 200, each missed fraud contributes 200× the gradient of a false positive. The model is therefore penalised heavily for missing fraud, driving it to increase recall at the expense of precision. This directly encodes the cost asymmetry into the training objective — the most principled way to handle it.

Primary source to read next

Dal Pozzolo et al. — "Calibrating Probability with Undersampling for Unbalanced Classification" (IEEE SSCI, 2015)

The foundational paper on threshold calibration and undersampling for fraud detection, using a real credit card fraud dataset. Shows empirically why accuracy fails and how to correctly calibrate model outputs for operational use. Widely cited in fraud ML literature.
