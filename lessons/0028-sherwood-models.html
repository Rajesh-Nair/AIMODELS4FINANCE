Lesson 28 · Retail Credit Strategy

Sherwood Models

The customer-level acquisition profitability framework — how retail banks and credit card lenders move beyond "will they default?" to "will we make money from this customer over the life of the relationship?"

About this lesson
The "Sherwood model" is a practitioner framework term used within retail banking and credit card operations — specifically for customer-level P&L-driven acquisition and portfolio management. It does not appear extensively in public academic literature by this name, which is consistent with proprietary internal model frameworks in financial services. This lesson is built from the definition: "a specialized credit risk and profitability framework used by retail banks and credit card lenders to analyze customer-level acquisition decisions, evaluate lifetime portfolio value, and optimize P&L impact for loans." The underlying methodology is well-documented in the profit-based credit underwriting literature.

1. The problem with purely risk-based acquisition

Traditional credit underwriting asks one question: will this applicant default? This is the PD model (Lesson 1). A risk-only acquisition strategy approves all applicants above a PD threshold and declines those below. The flaw:

  • Low-risk ≠ high-profit: the safest borrowers (highest credit scores) are typically the least profitable — they borrow modestly, pay on time, carry low balances, and generate little interest income. In credit cards, they may pay in full every month (transactors) rather than carrying a balance (revolvers).
  • High-risk ≠ unprofitable: moderate-risk customers generate significant interest income and fee revenue. If priced correctly and acquired at the right credit limit, their losses are more than offset by revenue.
  • Customer value depends on behaviour: the same credit score customer can be high-value (active revolving, product cross-sell, longevity) or low-value (early repayment, attrition, fraud) — PD alone does not predict this.

Sherwood models resolve this by replacing the single PD question with a full lifetime P&L forecast for each prospective customer.

2. The Sherwood model P&L decomposition

The core of a Sherwood model is a decomposition of expected lifetime P&L into its component revenue and cost streams, modelled at the individual customer level:

Revenue

Interest income (balance × APR)
Annual/monthly fees
Interchange fees (card transactions)
Late payment fees
FX transaction fees

Operating Costs

Acquisition cost (marketing, processing)
Servicing cost (statements, calls)
Rewards / cashback
Funding cost (cost of capital × balance)
Card network costs

Credit Losses

Expected loss = PD × LGD × EAD
Fraud losses
Recovery costs (collection spend)

Net P&L

Revenue
− Costs
− Losses
────────
= Lifetime NPV

Each component is a separate model, trained on the bank's own portfolio history. The outputs are combined into a single per-customer P&L forecast, which is then discounted to present value (NPV) over a projection horizon (typically 3–5 years for credit cards, up to the full loan term for personal loans).

3. The vintage model — the measurement backbone

The Sherwood model is calibrated using vintage analysis: tracking cohorts of customers acquired in the same period (vintage) through time, observing how their actual P&L evolves against the acquisition-time forecast.

Vintage analysis serves two purposes: (1) validates that the acquisition model's P&L forecasts are accurate, and (2) provides the training data for the next model refresh — linking applicant characteristics at acquisition to realised lifetime P&L.

5. Acquisition decision optimisation

The Sherwood model output — a distribution of forecast P&L per applicant — is used to optimise the acquisition decision. This goes beyond binary approve/decline:

  • Credit limit optimisation: the credit limit is a lever that affects balance ramp, default risk, and revolving income simultaneously. A higher limit increases interest income potential but also EAD. The optimal limit maximises expected P&L subject to credit risk constraints. This is typically solved by evaluating P&L over a grid of limit values and selecting the optimum per applicant segment.
  • Pricing optimisation: APR is another lever — higher rates generate more interest but deter revolving behaviour and increase default risk (adverse selection). ML demand elasticity models estimate the APR-behaviour relationship and feed into the P&L model.
  • Channel and offer personalisation: the acquisition channel (direct mail vs digital vs partner) and offer terms (0% introductory, cashback, rewards) affect the type of customer acquired — influencing which segment of the P&L model applies.
Acquisition decision: approve if E[Lifetime NPV(applicant)] > threshold
                     AND credit risk limits are not breached
                     AND fair lending constraints are satisfied

Credit limit: argmax_L { E[NPV(L)] } subject to: EAD × LGD ≤ risk appetite

6. Portfolio management and P&L optimisation

Sherwood frameworks extend beyond acquisition into in-life portfolio management — the ongoing decisions about existing customer accounts:

  • Proactive credit limit changes: increase limits for customers with high revolving utilisation and improving credit quality (more balance → more revenue); reduce limits for customers showing early delinquency signals (reducing EAD)
  • Retention offers: for customers showing attrition signals (low usage, competitor balance transfers), the model calculates the maximum retention spend (promotional rate, fee waiver) consistent with the remaining expected lifetime NPV
  • Collections strategy segmentation: for customers entering arrears, the Sherwood P&L lens determines the optimal collections approach — how much is it worth spending to recover a specific account, given its remaining expected P&L contribution?
  • Forbearance analysis: for customers requesting payment deferrals, the model forecasts lifetime P&L under different forbearance scenarios — supporting decisions about whether to offer forbearance and on what terms

7. Regulatory and fairness dimensions

Sherwood models carry the same fairness and regulatory obligations as any credit model:

  • Disparate impact testing: the four-fifths rule (Lesson 4) must be applied to approval rates and credit limit decisions across protected characteristics — even if the P&L model does not explicitly use protected characteristics, proxy features can create disparate impact
  • Consumer Duty (UK) / UDAP (US): P&L-optimised offers must still represent fair value for the customer (Lesson 27). A product that is highly profitable for the bank but harmful to the customer — through high-rate revolving debt that the customer cannot sustainably manage — may fail Consumer Duty
  • IFRS9 alignment: the credit loss component of the Sherwood model must be consistent with the IFRS9 ECL model (Lesson 12). A bank cannot have different PD estimates in its pricing model and its accounting provisioning model without documented justification (PIT vs TTC distinction applies)
  • SR 11-7 / MRM: the Sherwood framework constitutes a material model for credit decisions. Full model risk management lifecycle (development, validation, ongoing monitoring, PSI, vintage tracking) is required
The P&L optimisation trap
A Sherwood model that perfectly optimises the bank's expected P&L can still produce bad outcomes if it (a) disproportionately targets financially vulnerable customers who are profitable but at risk of debt spiral; (b) prices in a way that disparately affects protected groups; or (c) uses the model to justify predatory credit limit increases to customers showing stress signals. UK Consumer Duty and US CFPB oversight specifically scrutinise whether profitability models have been used to target vulnerable customers. Governance frameworks must include both P&L optimisation objectives AND explicit customer outcome constraints — the model optimises within the space of outcomes that are both profitable and fair.
Retrieval Practice

1. A credit card applicant has an excellent credit score (low PD). Why might a Sherwood model assign them a lower expected lifetime NPV than a moderate-risk applicant?

Correct. This is the fundamental insight that motivates profit-based underwriting. The credit card P&L is non-linear in risk: at very low risk (high score), the customer borrows safely but generates little interest income — they're "transactors" who pay in full. At very high risk, losses overwhelm any revenue. Maximum profit occurs at a middle risk level — the customer revolves a balance, pays regularly but not in full, generating interest income that more than covers expected losses at the right APR. A pure risk model would approve the low-risk customer eagerly and hesitate on the moderate-risk one; a Sherwood model may reverse this prioritisation.
Credit card economics are non-monotone in credit quality. The most credit-worthy customers are often transactors: they pay their full balance monthly, generating interchange fees but zero interest income. The bank's most profitable customers carry a balance (revolvers) — they pay interest month after month, well in excess of expected credit losses at a properly-priced APR. A pure risk model maximises approval quality but misses the revenue-risk trade-off. Sherwood models capture this by modelling the revolve propensity and balance trajectory separately from the default probability — allowing credit limit and pricing decisions that optimise the complete P&L picture.

2. A Sherwood model recommends increasing the credit limit for an existing customer whose utilisation has been consistently at 85% and who has never missed a payment. What is the intended P&L impact, and what risk does this create?

Correct. 85% utilisation + clean payment history = a reliable, high-balance revolver. This customer is generating maximum interest income per pound of limit. Increasing the limit allows the balance to grow — more interest income. The model expects the customer to "ramp up" their balance into the new headroom over 3–6 months, following the balance ramp trajectory learned from similar customers. The counterparty risk is that if this customer defaults after the limit increase, EAD is larger. The Sherwood model must verify that the incremental NPV of the extra balance exceeds the incremental expected loss from higher EAD.
A consistently revolving customer at 85% utilisation, never missing a payment, is one of the most profitable customer profiles: they carry a large balance, pay reliably, and have demonstrated ability to manage this level of debt. The Sherwood model identifies that giving this customer a higher limit will — based on similar customers' behaviour — lead to a higher balance, generating more interest income. The risk: higher EAD if they later default. The model's job is to verify that E[incremental revenue] > E[incremental loss] before recommending the increase. This is a risk-reward optimisation, not a credit approval — and the distinction matters for governance.

3. A bank's Sherwood acquisition model and its IFRS9 PD model produce different probability of default estimates for the same applicant population. What are the legitimate reasons for this difference, and when does it become a governance problem?

Correct. PIT vs TTC is the primary legitimate reason for PD differences between acquisition models and IFRS9 models. The Sherwood acquisition model uses a PIT PD — calibrated to current economic conditions — because the pricing decision is being made today. IFRS9 uses a hybrid or TTC PD because provisioning must cover expected losses across the economic cycle (IFRS9 Stage 1 ECL uses 12-month PD; Stage 2/3 use lifetime PD). The governance requirement is documentation: the bank must be able to explain the difference to regulators, validators, and auditors without suggesting that the models are inconsistent in a problematic way. Unexplained large differences between pricing PDs and provisioning PDs are a red flag in model risk reviews.
PIT PD vs TTC PD is the documented legitimate reason for acquisition vs provisioning PD differences. Sherwood acquisition pricing uses PIT PD (current conditions → pricing should reflect today's risk, not a 10-year average). IFRS9 ECL uses hybrid PD adjusted for the economic cycle (IFRS9 Stage 1 expects 12-month horizon ECL; Stage 2/3 extend this). A bank with a PIT PD of 1.5% in Sherwood and a TTC-equivalent PD of 2.5% in IFRS9 during a benign period is behaving consistently — the provisioning model is conservatively reflecting the full cycle. Without documentation, the same difference could suggest the pricing model is underestimating losses to justify a higher-NPV approval rate — which is exactly what regulators look for in model comparisons.
