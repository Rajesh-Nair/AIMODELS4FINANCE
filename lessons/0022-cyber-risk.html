Lesson 22 · Operational & Cyber Risk

Cyber Risk Models

From qualitative risk registers to quantitative cyber loss distributions — why cyber is the hardest operational risk to model, and how ML is helping.

1. Cyber risk in the financial risk taxonomy

Cyber risk sits within operational risk (Basel defines op risk as "risk of loss from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems, or from external events"). It has become systemically important: the IMF estimated in 2024 that cyber attacks on financial institutions caused over $12bn in direct losses in the prior decade, with indirect costs (reputational, regulatory) far larger.

What makes cyber risk distinctive as a modelling problem:

  • Data scarcity: cyber loss events are rare; firms rarely disclose full loss quantum; few cyber events have occurred at the extreme tail
  • Adversarial nature: unlike credit or market risk, an attacker adapts to defences — the loss frequency and severity distribution shifts as the threat landscape evolves
  • Correlation: a single successful attack (e.g., a zero-day on widely-used banking software) can affect multiple institutions simultaneously — systemic correlation risk
  • Fat tails: the largest cyber events dwarf typical losses; the distribution is extremely heavy-tailed

2. Loss Distribution Approach (LDA) for cyber risk

The classical actuarial tool for operational risk is the Loss Distribution Approach, which separately models loss frequency and severity, then computes the aggregate loss distribution by Monte Carlo convolution:

N ~ Poisson(λ) or Negative Binomial    [cyber event frequency per year]
X_i ~ LogNormal(μ, σ) or Pareto(α)     [individual cyber event severity]

Aggregate annual loss: S = Σᵢ₌₁ᴺ Xᵢ

VaR(99.9%) of S → Op Risk / Cyber Capital requirement

The critical parameters are λ (attack frequency) and the severity distribution. For cyber: severity is Pareto-like (extremely fat tail) and λ is institution-specific, varying with security posture, industry sector, and firm size.

External data supplementation: Because own-firm cyber loss history is sparse, LDA calibration relies on industry databases (ORX, Advisen CyberAwareness, CISSM) supplemented by scenario analysis. ML improves frequency estimation by incorporating threat intelligence feeds, security control effectiveness scores, and attacker activity levels.

3. The FAIR framework for cyber risk quantification

FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk) is an alternative to LDA that decomposes cyber loss into a causal chain:

Risk = f(Threat Event Frequency, Vulnerability, Loss Event Frequency,
         Loss Magnitude)

Where:
  Threat Event Frequency = how often does a threat actor attempt an attack?
  Vulnerability         = given an attempt, probability of success?
  Loss Magnitude        = if successful, what is the financial impact?

FAIR uses Monte Carlo with probability distributions on each factor
→ outputs a loss exceedance curve

ML integration with FAIR: NLP on threat intelligence reports estimates threat event frequency by attacker type; ML on vulnerability scan data estimates vulnerability by system; historical incident data trains the loss magnitude distribution. FAIR provides a more explainable causal structure than pure LDA — useful for communicating cyber risk to boards and regulators.

4. Scenario analysis for extreme cyber events

Tail cyber events — a coordinated attack on the financial sector, ransomware encrypting core banking systems, a SWIFT network compromise — are too rare to estimate from loss history. Scenario analysis fills the gap:

  • CBEST (UK) / TIBER-EU: regulator-mandated threat intelligence-led penetration testing. The bank simulates a real attack by an intelligence-grade adversary; the results inform the realistic maximum loss scenario.
  • Tabletop exercises: structured scenarios (data breach, cloud provider outage, ransomware) used to estimate operational response time and cost.
  • DORA (EU Digital Operational Resilience Act, 2025): mandates financial institutions to test and document operational resilience against cyber and IT failures. Threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) is required for significant institutions.

6. Cyber insurance capital under Solvency II / Basel

Banks hold capital for operational risk (including cyber) under Basel III. The two approaches:

  • Standardised approach (SA): op risk capital = 12.5% × Bank's Business Indicator (a proxy for income size). Simple, no model. Cyber specifics not captured.
  • Internal Model / AMA (Advanced Measurement Approach — now discontinued in Basel IV but preserved in US/UK for some banks): LDA or scenario-based model. More risk-sensitive but subject to full MRM governance and regulatory approval.

Cyber insurance policies (first-party and third-party) transfer cyber risk to the insurance market. ML pricing of cyber insurance policies uses the same LDA/FAIR frameworks — the insurer needs to estimate the loss distribution for the policyholder's specific risk profile.

DORA and model governance for cyber
The EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA, effective January 2025) requires financial institutions to demonstrate that their ICT systems — including models and model infrastructure — are resilient against cyber and IT disruption. This creates a new governance dimension for ML models: the model deployment infrastructure (cloud VMs, ML serving endpoints, data pipelines) must be included in the DORA operational resilience testing scope. A model that is mathematically sound but deployed on unpatched infrastructure fails DORA requirements.
Retrieval Practice

1. Why does external cyber loss data (from industry databases like ORX) produce uncertain LDA calibrations even when large datasets are available?

Correct. External cyber data has three structural problems: (1) underreporting — firms disclose the minimum required, so loss data is negatively biased; (2) survivorship bias — catastrophic losses may cause firm failure, removing those events from the database; (3) non-stationarity — a five-year-old loss distribution does not reflect today's attacker capabilities, toolkits, or target selection. ML threat intelligence models help address (3) by providing near-real-time frequency estimates, but (1) and (2) remain inherent limitations of any data-based approach.
Cyber data problems compound: firms disclose the minimum required by regulation, so the external database underestimates true loss frequency and quantum. Attacks that cause firm failure don't add to the database — survivorship bias tilts toward survivable events. Most critically, the cyber threat landscape in 2019 is different from 2024 — ransomware tactics, zero-days, and AI-assisted attacks evolve faster than a historical loss dataset can capture. These three biases mean external data is a starting point, not a reliable tail estimator.

2. In the FAIR model, what is the distinction between "threat event frequency" and "loss event frequency"?

Correct. FAIR decomposes frequency explicitly: many attack attempts occur; most are blocked by defences. Loss event frequency = threat event frequency × (1 − probability of successfully blocking). This decomposition is valuable because it separates the attacker's behaviour (threat frequency) from the defender's effectiveness (vulnerability). ML can estimate each separately: NLP on threat intel for frequency, ML on security control data for vulnerability. Improving defences reduces loss event frequency without changing how often attackers try.
FAIR's causal structure is: attacks happen (threat event frequency) → some succeed given the institution's defences (vulnerability) → a successful attack causes losses (loss magnitude). Loss event frequency = threat event frequency × P(success | attempt). This decomposition matters practically: a bank facing 1,000 attacks per day with 0.01% success rate has the same loss event frequency as one facing 100 attacks with 0.1% success. But the management response is entirely different — one is about reducing attack surface, the other about improving defences.

3. How does DORA expand the scope of operational resilience testing beyond what Basel III op risk capital alone requires?

Correct. Basel III op risk capital (and LDA models) quantifies the capital needed if cyber losses occur. DORA takes a complementary approach: it requires firms to demonstrate they can prevent or recover from cyber events — through TLPT testing, ICT resilience frameworks, third-party risk management, and incident reporting. These are operational controls requirements, not capital adequacy requirements. Both are needed: capital for when things go wrong; operational resilience to reduce how often and how severely things go wrong.
Basel III op risk capital is a financial buffer: hold enough capital to absorb cyber losses at the 99.9% confidence level. DORA is about preventing or rapidly recovering from those losses: threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) proves defences work against sophisticated adversaries; ICT resilience requirements prove the firm can recover critical services within defined timeframes. These are complementary — DORA reduces λ (attack success frequency) in your LDA; Basel capital ensures the firm survives if λ outcomes materialise.
