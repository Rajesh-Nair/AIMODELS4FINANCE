Lesson 01 · Credit Risk

PD Modelling: From Scorecards to XGBoost

How ML improves default prediction — and what the regulator demands in return.

1. What is PD and why does it dominate credit risk?

Probability of Default (PD) is the estimated likelihood that a borrower will fail to meet a contractual debt obligation within a defined horizon — typically 12 months (Basel point-in-time) or through-the-cycle (TTC). PD is one of three key risk parameters mandated under the Basel III / IV framework for banks using the Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach:

Parameter Full name What it estimates
PD Probability of Default Likelihood the borrower defaults within 12 months
LGD Loss Given Default Fraction of exposure lost if default occurs
EAD Exposure at Default Outstanding balance the bank faces at moment of default

Expected credit loss = PD × LGD × EAD. Banks use PD to calculate Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA), which directly determines how much capital must be held. A more accurate PD model means more precise capital allocation — either freeing capital for lending or correctly buffering against loss.

Regulatory context
Under Basel IRB Advanced, banks estimate PD themselves (subject to regulatory floors and validation). Under the Standardised Approach, PD is prescribed by the regulator. IRB approval is a competitive advantage — and a governance burden.

2. The classic approach: logistic regression scorecard

For decades, banks used logistic regression scorecards as the PD model of record. The approach is well-understood by regulators and auditors and has specific properties that make it durable in a regulated environment:

  • Monotonic relationships between features and PD (higher debt = higher PD)
  • Full interpretability — each variable has a coefficient that maps directly to a log-odds contribution
  • Weight of Evidence (WoE) encoding — continuous variables are binned, making outliers and missings explicit
  • Output is a calibrated probability in [0, 1], directly usable as PD
Why scorecards survive
A logistic scorecard passes challenger–champion testing trivially. A regulator can look at the model card, read the coefficients, and immediately verify business logic (e.g., "more missed payments → higher PD"). No black-box concerns. This interpretability premium is why scorecards still dominate retail lending even as XGBoost beats them on AUC.

3. Where XGBoost and neural networks win

Traditional scorecards assume roughly monotonic, additive relationships between features and default. Real borrower behaviour is messier:

  • A high income borrower with very high spending and very high debt may default at a disproportionately high rate — an interaction the additive model cannot capture cleanly
  • The relationship between credit utilisation and default is non-linear: low utilisation (0–30%) is safe, 30–70% is moderate risk, >90% signals distress — a step function, not a line
  • Alternative data (transaction patterns, device metadata, bureau tradelines) has complex multi-way interactions

BIS Working Paper 765 (2019) compared ML methods to logistic regression across real consumer loan portfolios and found consistent Gini improvements of 3–8 percentage points for gradient boosted trees, primarily from capturing non-linear interaction effects.

Feature types used in ML PD models

Feature category Examples ML advantage
Traditional bureau data Credit score, number of delinquencies, age of oldest account Non-linear thresholds, interaction with income
Application data Loan-to-income, employment tenure, product type Interaction effects between multiple features
Behavioural / transaction Average monthly balance, payment pattern, ATM usage Temporal patterns; high-dimensional feature space
Macro / contextual Unemployment rate, house price index, rate environment Regime detection, stress sensitivity

4. How PD models are evaluated

Credit risk has its own evaluation vocabulary. You need to know these; they appear in model validation reports, regulatory submissions, and risk committee packs.

Discrimination metrics

Metric What it measures Typical threshold
Gini coefficient How well the model separates defaulters from non-defaulters. Gini = 2 × AUC − 1 >50% acceptable; >70% strong for retail
AUC-ROC Area under the ROC curve — probability the model ranks a defaulter above a non-defaulter >0.75 for most retail portfolios
KS statistic Maximum separation between cumulative default and non-default distributions across score bands >40% acceptable; KS at score band used for cutoff setting
Lift / CAP curve Cumulative Accuracy Profile — used in regulatory validation submissions AR (Accuracy Ratio) = Gini; same measure, different name

Calibration metrics

Discrimination tells you whether the model ranks borrowers correctly. Calibration tells you whether the predicted PD is the right number. Both matter for capital calculation.

  • Hosmer–Lemeshow test: tests whether observed defaults match predicted PDs across score bins
  • Binomial test: at the portfolio level, tests whether the realised default rate is consistent with predicted PD
  • Traffic light approach (Basel): green/amber/red zones based on how far realised defaults deviate from predicted PD
Risk governance note
A model with great Gini but poor calibration will still cause capital miscalculation. Regulators evaluate both. XGBoost's raw output is not a calibrated probability — you must post-process it with Platt scaling or isotonic regression before treating it as PD. This is a common model risk finding.

5. The explainability problem — and SHAP

The core tension in ML-based PD modelling: better discrimination vs. regulatory interpretability. Regulators (PRA, ECB, OCC) require banks to be able to explain individual credit decisions — a requirement that stems from consumer protection law (GDPR, ECOA) as much as prudential regulation.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) has become the industry-standard response. For each prediction, SHAP decomposes the model output into per-feature contributions rooted in game-theoretic Shapley values: 

Predicted log-odds = base_rate
                   + SHAP(credit_utilisation) = +0.42
                   + SHAP(missed_payments)    = +0.71
                   + SHAP(loan_to_income)     = +0.18
                   + SHAP(employment_tenure)  = -0.15
                   + ...

This allows:

  • Adverse action notices ("your application was declined primarily due to high credit utilisation")
  • Model validation teams to verify that SHAP rankings are business-sensible
  • Regulators to audit individual decisions without needing to inspect the model internals

The best reference for SHAP theory and application is Christoph Molnar's Interpretable Machine Learning, Chapter 9 — freely available online.

6. Risk governance: what SR 11-7 demands

SR 11-7 (Federal Reserve, 2011) and the UK equivalent PRA SS1/23 define a model risk management (MRM) framework. For any PD model, this means:

MRM requirement What it means in practice for an XGBoost PD model
Model definition The model must be formally inventoried with a risk tier (High/Medium/Low). XGBoost PD for IRBA typically = High tier.
Development standards Training/test split, out-of-time validation, feature justification, no look-ahead bias. Documented in a Model Development Document (MDD).
Independent validation A team independent of development must replicate and stress-test the model. They produce a Model Validation Report (MVR) with findings and conditions.
Model approval A Model Risk Committee (MRC) approves the model for use. Any unresolved findings = conditions or limitations on use.
Ongoing monitoring Quarterly/annual performance monitoring reports tracking Gini, KS, PSI (Population Stability Index), and calibration. Triggers for review or redevelopment.
Common MRM finding for ML models
"The model development team did not adequately demonstrate that the selected hyperparameters (e.g., max_depth, n_estimators) are stable across different training windows and do not overfit to the development sample." — XGBoost's flexibility is a validation red flag without rigorous cross-validation and out-of-time testing.

Retrieval Practice

1. A PD model has AUC = 0.82. What is its Gini coefficient?

Correct. Gini = 2 × AUC − 1 = 2 × 0.82 − 1 = 0.64. This is a useful Gini for a retail PD model.
Not quite. The relationship is Gini = 2 × AUC − 1. So: 2 × 0.82 − 1 = 0.64.

2. Why does XGBoost require a calibration step before its output can be used as PD?

Correct. XGBoost's leaf scores are not calibrated probabilities — they rank well, but the actual values may not match observed default rates. Platt scaling or isotonic regression maps them onto the probability space.
Not quite. XGBoost's raw leaf output is a score that ranks borrowers well, but is not a calibrated probability. The actual numerical values won't match observed default rates without post-processing (Platt scaling or isotonic regression).

3. Under SR 11-7 / PRA SS1/23, who must validate a High-tier PD model before it goes into production?

Correct. SR 11-7 requires independence between model development and model validation. An internal team works, but it must be organisationally separate from the developers to avoid conflicts of interest.
SR 11-7 is explicit: the validation function must be independent from the model development function. The same team cannot validate their own model. External audit is one route, but most banks maintain an internal independent validation team.

4. A PD model has Gini = 68% but the Hosmer–Lemeshow test shows significant deviation in the top score band. What should happen?

Correct. Discrimination and calibration are separate requirements. A miscalibrated model will misprice risk even if its ranking is excellent. The standard remediation is recalibration or a conservative scalar add-on, documented as a model limitation.
Good Gini does not compensate for poor calibration. Calibration determines whether the PD number is accurate, not just the ranking. A miscalibrated model miscalculates RWA and capital. The response is a calibration finding in the MVR, followed by recalibration or a documented add-on.

5. SHAP values in an XGBoost PD model are used primarily to:

Correct. SHAP decomposes each prediction into additive feature contributions, enabling adverse action explanations, regulatory audit, and model validation business-sense checks.
SHAP doesn't change the model or its Gini — it explains predictions after the fact. Each prediction is decomposed into a sum of per-feature contributions, enabling adverse action notices and regulatory explainability.
