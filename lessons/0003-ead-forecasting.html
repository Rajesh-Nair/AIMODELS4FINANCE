EAD Forecasting: The Panic Draw

Why borrowers draw down credit lines just before default — and how ML models this adverse behaviour.

1. Completing the trinity: what EAD is

EAD — Exposure at Default — is the third parameter in the Basel expected loss formula. It answers: how much does the bank actually owe us at the moment the borrower defaults?

EL = PD × LGD × EAD EAD = amount the bank is exposed to at the point of default

For a fixed-term loan (e.g., a term mortgage), EAD is straightforward: it is the outstanding principal balance at default, which follows a known amortisation schedule. There is little to model.

The challenge arises with revolving facilities: credit cards, overdrafts, revolving credit facilities (RCFs), and undrawn loan commitments. Here, the borrower controls how much they draw. The outstanding balance can change daily — and, critically, borrowers behave very differently when they are approaching distress.

2. The Credit Conversion Factor (CCF)

For revolving facilities, the EAD is decomposed into two components:

EAD = Current Outstanding + CCF × Undrawn Commitment CCF (Credit Conversion Factor) = fraction of the undrawn limit expected to be drawn by default

Example: A borrower has a £10,000 credit card limit. Current balance is £4,000 (40% utilised). Undrawn commitment = £6,000. If CCF = 0.5, the model predicts:

EAD = £4,000 + 0.5 × £6,000 = £7,000

Under the Basel Foundation IRB approach, the regulator prescribes CCF values (e.g., 75% for undrawn revolving commitments). Under Advanced IRB, banks estimate their own CCF using historical data — and this is where ML enters.

3. The panic draw: adverse selection in revolving facilities

The core phenomenon that makes EAD modelling non-trivial is adverse selection in drawdown behaviour. Distressed borrowers systematically draw on their credit lines before defaulting:

This creates a PD–EAD correlation analogous to the PD–LGD correlation from Lesson 2: the borrowers most likely to default are also those who draw most heavily before default. A model that ignores this correlation will systematically underestimate EAD for the riskiest accounts.

Capital implication
If your EAD model is built on average CCF across the whole portfolio, it underestimates EAD for high-PD segments. Since EL = PD × LGD × EAD, this compounds with PD and LGD errors in precisely the accounts that drive the most risk. Basel's Advanced IRB requires banks to use CCF estimates that reflect this drawdown behaviour — not just population-average CCFs.

4. How ML models EAD

EAD / CCF modelling with ML is less standardised than PD or LGD — fewer banks have full AIRB approval for EAD. The primary approaches:

Approach 1: Regression on CCF

Treat CCF as a continuous target (bounded [0, 1] for the undrawn portion). Apply XGBoost or Random Forest regression, with features that capture behavioural signals. The same bimodal problem as LGD can appear (CCF = 0 for accounts that pay down before default; CCF near 1 for panic draws), making two-stage approaches useful here too.

Approach 2: Utilisation trajectory modelling

Rather than predicting CCF at a single point, model the path of utilisation from the reference date to default. This is naturally suited to recurrent neural networks (LSTM) or gradient boosted models with time-lagged features. The sequence of monthly utilisation changes carries strong signal about imminent default.

Approach 3: Cohort / segment models

For portfolios with limited data, segment accounts into behavioural cohorts (e.g., by current utilisation band and product type) and estimate average CCF per cohort. This is more robust than individual-level regression when training data is sparse but loses the precision of account-level prediction.

6. The reference date problem

EAD modelling has a reference date issue that PD and LGD models do not. CCF measures how much additional drawing occurs between a reference date and the date of default. The choice of reference date materially affects the estimated CCF:

  • 12-month reference: CCF measured from 12 months before default. Captures the full panic draw trajectory. Matches the standard 12-month PD horizon.
  • Fixed point (e.g., last performing date): CCF measured from the last date the account was performing. Accounts for the exact pre-default window but varies in length across accounts.
Validation requirement
Model validators must verify that the reference date choice is consistent between the CCF model development sample and how the model is applied in production. A mismatch — e.g., CCF trained on 12-month reference but applied at the current date for accounts at various stages — produces systematic bias in EAD estimates.

Retrieval Practice

1. A borrower has a £20,000 revolving credit limit, current outstanding £8,000, and the bank estimates CCF = 0.60. What is the EAD?

Correct. Undrawn = £20,000 − £8,000 = £12,000. EAD = £8,000 + 0.60 × £12,000 = £8,000 + £7,200 = £15,200.
EAD = Current Outstanding + CCF × Undrawn. Undrawn = £20,000 − £8,000 = £12,000. EAD = £8,000 + 0.60 × £12,000 = £15,200.

2. Why is the "panic draw" phenomenon a problem for EAD models trained on population-average CCFs?

Correct. This is PD–EAD correlation: the accounts most likely to default are the same accounts that draw most aggressively before default. Population-average CCFs wash out this adverse selection, systematically underestimating EAD for high-risk segments.
PD and EAD are positively correlated — the most distressed borrowers (high PD) draw most heavily before defaulting. A population-average CCF averages over both low-risk (low-draw) and high-risk (high-draw) accounts, producing a CCF that is too low for precisely the accounts that matter most for capital.

3. Under Basel Foundation IRB, how is CCF typically set?

Correct. Foundation IRB uses regulatory prescribed CCFs. Advanced IRB is the approach that allows (and requires) banks to estimate their own CCFs — which is where ML modelling becomes relevant.
Foundation IRB uses regulator-prescribed CCFs. Advanced IRB permits banks to estimate their own — that is when ML modelling of CCF becomes relevant and valuable.

4. A bank's EAD model is trained using CCFs measured from 12 months before default. In production, the model is applied at the current date for all accounts. What is the risk?

Correct. The CCF definition in development (12 months before default) must match how it is applied. If the model is applied at the current date for accounts at varying stages, the implied look-ahead horizon differs from the training definition — a reference date inconsistency that validators always check.
This is a reference date inconsistency. The training data defines CCF as the drawdown in the 12 months before default. But in production, the model is applied at the current date for accounts that may be 3 months, 6 months, or 24 months away from default. The horizon mismatch produces biased EAD estimates — a classic model risk finding for revolving facility models.
