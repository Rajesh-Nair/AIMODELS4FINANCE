Lesson 04 · Credit Risk

Credit Scoring with Alternative Data

How ensemble methods unlock thin-file borrowers — and the fair lending risk hiding inside every new feature.

1. The credit scoring problem

Credit scoring sits at the top of the credit decision funnel: before a loan is originated, the bank must decide whether to lend, at what rate, and at what limit. Scoring models assign each applicant a number that maps to credit risk, enabling automated decisions at scale.

Classical credit scoring used a narrow feature set: payment history, outstanding balances, length of credit history, types of credit, and new credit inquiries (the FICO five-factor framework). Ensemble ML models shatter this constraint — they can process hundreds of features from multiple data sources, finding signals invisible to a logistic scorecard.

The thin-file problem
Around 45 million US adults are "credit invisible" — they have no, or too little, credit history for traditional scoring. Alternative data promises to extend credit access to thin-file and no-file borrowers. The opportunity is real; so is the risk of encoding discrimination into the model in new ways.

2. Data taxonomy: traditional vs alternative

Traditional bureau data

  • Payment history (on-time, late, delinquent)
  • Revolving utilisation rates
  • Number and age of tradelines
  • Hard inquiry count (new credit seeking)
  • Derogatory marks (bankruptcies, collections)

Alternative: transaction data

  • Monthly income / spending patterns
  • Merchant category mix (gambling, healthcare, etc.)
  • Payday loan usage, cash advance frequency
  • Rent and utility payment history
  • Account balance volatility, overdraft frequency

Alternative: behavioural / digital

  • App usage patterns (loan-app navigation)
  • Device metadata (OS version, phone model)
  • Time-of-day application patterns
  • Form fill speed and corrections
  • Social media activity (some jurisdictions)

Alternative: telco / utility

  • Mobile phone payment history
  • Data/voice plan type and tenure
  • Electricity / gas payment regularity
  • Geolocation consistency (work/home patterns)

3. Why ensembles outperform scorecards on rich feature sets

With a small, curated feature set, the interpretability advantage of logistic regression is worth the discrimination trade-off. As the feature space grows to hundreds of variables, the ensemble advantages compound:

  • Automatic interaction detection: transaction mix × balance volatility × income regularity captures distress signals no human analyst would construct manually
  • Non-linear thresholds: spending on gambling sites below £50/month may be harmless; above £200/month in a low-income account is a default predictor — a cliff the logistic model cannot represent without manual binning
  • Robustness to irrelevant features: Random Forest's feature sampling at each split and XGBoost's regularisation both act as automatic feature selectors, preventing noise features from degrading performance
  • Missing value handling: tree-based models handle missing features via surrogate splits, reducing the need for imputation that can introduce its own bias

4. Feature importance and selection

With hundreds of features, interpretability requires deliberate tools. Three are standard:

Gain-based importance (XGBoost native)

Ranks features by the average reduction in loss when each feature is used as a split. Fast and built-in, but biased toward high-cardinality features (continuous variables get more split opportunities than binary ones). Use for initial ranking; do not rely on it alone.

Permutation importance

Shuffle each feature's values and measure the drop in model performance. Model-agnostic, less biased than gain importance, and more interpretable ("removing this feature costs 2pp Gini"). Computationally heavier; worth running on the final feature set.

SHAP feature importance

Average |SHAP value| per feature across the dataset — the most rigorous of the three. SHAP also enables individual-level decomposition for adverse action explanations, which is required by consumer protection law. The SHAP beeswarm plot (distribution of SHAP values per feature across all applicants) is now standard in model validation packs.

Practical note
In a real credit scoring engagement, you will typically start with 200–400 candidate features, run SHAP importance and correlation analysis, and whittle to 20–40 variables for the final model. The target: maximum discrimination with minimum feature count, to reduce maintenance burden and proxy variable risk.

5. The proxy variable problem

This is the central fair lending risk in ML credit scoring. Proxy variables are features that are correlated with a protected characteristic (race, gender, religion, national origin, age) even though they do not mention it directly. Feeding them into a model encodes discrimination without a single discriminatory intent.

6. Fair lending governance: disparate impact analysis

In the US, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) and Fair Housing Act prohibit both intentional discrimination (disparate treatment) and facially neutral policies that produce discriminatory outcomes (disparate impact). The UK equivalent is the Equality Act 2010. GDPR Article 22 adds a right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects.

For ML credit models, the standard governance response is a disparate impact analysis:

  1. Obtain protected class data (or infer it using BISG — Bayesian Improved Surname Geocoding)
  2. Compare approval rates and pricing across protected groups
  3. Apply the four-fifths rule: if the approval rate for a protected group is less than 80% of the rate for the highest-approved group, prima facie disparate impact exists
  4. Test whether removing suspected proxy features reduces the disparity
  5. Document the business necessity justification for any remaining disparity
The explainability–fairness tension
Removing proxy variables reduces disparate impact but also reduces model Gini — you lose predictive signal. Banks must document this trade-off explicitly. The regulator expects to see: (a) what features were removed for fairness reasons, (b) what Gini was sacrificed, and (c) evidence the remaining model still meets credit policy objectives. Governance documentation is as important as the model itself.

7. Model governance for credit scoring ML

Governance requirementML-specific consideration
Feature inventory Every feature must be documented: source, definition, refresh frequency, missing value rate. Alternative data sources often have unstable feeds — a feature available 98% of the time in development may degrade in production.
Adverse action notices Consumer protection law requires applicants to receive the top reasons for denial. SHAP values must be translated into human-readable reasons and reviewed for regulatory acceptability.
Population Stability Index (PSI) Monitors distributional shift between development population and production. PSI > 0.25 typically triggers model review. Alternative data features are particularly prone to PSI drift (e.g., if a data supplier changes methodology).
Disparate impact monitoring Not a one-time check — must be monitored quarterly in production as the population mix and feature distributions evolve.
Model card A structured document (Google's Model Card format, or regulatory equivalent) capturing intended use, performance metrics, fairness assessments, and known limitations. Required by some regulators and increasingly expected by all.
Retrieval Practice

1. A credit scoring model uses postcode as a feature. What specific fair lending risk does this introduce?

Correct. Postcode is a classic proxy variable for race/ethnicity in many markets. Residential segregation means geographic location carries demographic signal, and a model using postcode may produce disparate impact even without any intent to discriminate.
This is the proxy variable problem. Postcode correlates with race/ethnicity due to residential segregation — using it in the model can produce disparate impact outcomes even though race is never explicitly included. Regulators specifically flag geographic features as proxy risks.

2. The four-fifths rule flags disparate impact when a protected group's approval rate falls below what threshold relative to the highest-approved group?

Correct. The four-fifths (80%) rule: if a protected group's selection rate is less than 80% of the highest group's rate, prima facie adverse impact is indicated. This triggers investigation and justification, not automatic disqualification of the model.
The four-fifths rule sets the threshold at 80%. If any protected group's approval rate is less than 80% of the highest-approved group's rate, disparate impact analysis is triggered. It is a flag for investigation, not an automatic model disqualifier.

3. SHAP values are used in credit scoring adverse action notices to:

Correct. SHAP decomposes each prediction into per-feature contributions. For a denial, the features with the largest positive SHAP values (pushing toward high risk) become the adverse action reasons — translated into consumer-friendly language like "high credit card utilisation" or "insufficient payment history."
SHAP enables adverse action notices by identifying which features drove a specific denial. The top contributors (highest |SHAP|) are translated into human-readable decline reasons required by ECOA. This is why SHAP has become the de facto explainability standard for consumer credit ML models.

4. Population Stability Index (PSI) > 0.25 in production monitoring indicates:

Correct. PSI measures the distributional shift of the model score (or individual features) between the development population and the current population. PSI > 0.25 is the conventional trigger for model review — the population has drifted enough that the model's development assumptions may no longer hold.
PSI (Population Stability Index) measures distributional shift. It compares the score distribution at development time vs. the current production population. PSI > 0.25 = significant shift, typically triggering model review. Alternative data features are especially prone to PSI drift if data supplier methodology changes.
