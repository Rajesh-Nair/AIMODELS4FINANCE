Lesson 05 · Market Risk

VaR & ES: Fat Tails and ML

Why parametric approaches systematically underestimate tail risk — and how ML fills the gap.

1. What VaR and ES measure

Market risk asks: how much could this portfolio lose over a given horizon at a given confidence level? Two measures dominate:

MeasureDefinitionRegulatory use
VaR (Value at Risk) The loss that will not be exceeded with probability c over horizon h. At 99% confidence / 10-day horizon: there is a 1% chance of losing more than VaR. Basel II / 2.5 internal model capital charge. Now superseded under FRTB but still widely used for internal limits.
ES (Expected Shortfall) The expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR. Averages the tail beyond the confidence level. Also called CVaR (Conditional VaR). FRTB (Basel III / IV): replaces VaR as the primary capital measure. ES at 97.5% replaces VaR at 99%.

The key difference: VaR tells you the threshold you'll breach 1% of days. ES tells you the average loss on those bad days. ES is sub-additive (rewards diversification) and captures the shape of the tail — making it a better measure of capital adequacy.

2. Why the normal distribution assumption fails

Parametric VaR assumes returns are normally distributed. This is violated in financial markets in two systematic ways:

Fat tails (leptokurtosis): Actual return distributions have more mass in the extremes than the normal. Extreme losses happen 5–10× more often than the normal distribution predicts.

Volatility clustering: Calm periods cluster with calm periods; turbulent periods cluster with turbulent. Large losses on consecutive days violate the IID assumption.

The 2008 financial crisis made this concrete. Many banks using parametric VaR experienced losses that were nominally "25-sigma events" — events the normal model said should occur once in billions of years. They occurred on consecutive trading days. The failure was not bad luck; it was a wrong model.

What parametric VaR gets wrong
At 99% confidence, normal-distribution VaR underestimates tail losses by 30–60% for typical equity portfolios and by far more for options books and credit portfolios. The fatter and more skewed the actual return distribution, the larger the underestimate.

3. The three VaR methodologies — before ML

Parametric (variance-covariance) VaR

Assumes normally distributed returns. VaR = μ − zα × σ × √h, where zα is the normal quantile. Fast to compute; wrong for fat tails and non-linear portfolios (options).

Historical simulation (HS)

Replays actual historical P&L scenarios from a lookback window (typically 250–500 days). VaR = the empirical quantile of the P&L distribution. No distribution assumption — the fat tails are captured automatically from history. Limitation: only as good as the history. If a scenario hasn't appeared in the lookback window, the model is blind to it.

Monte Carlo simulation

Generates thousands of synthetic return paths from an assumed multivariate distribution. Flexible but expensive; dependent on how the distributional assumptions are specified.

4. GARCH: capturing volatility clustering

GARCH (Generalised AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) models time-varying volatility directly. The GARCH(1,1) specification is the workhorse: 

σ²_t = ω  +  α × ε²_{t-1}  +  β × σ²_{t-1}

where:
  σ²_t   = conditional variance at time t
  ε_{t-1} = yesterday's return shock
  α      = reaction to shocks (ARCH term)
  β      = persistence of volatility (GARCH term)
  α + β  < 1 for stationarity (typically 0.97–0.99)

Filtered Historical Simulation (FHS) combines GARCH with HS: standardise historical returns by their GARCH-estimated volatility (zt = εt / σt), giving IID standardised residuals. Then scale the simulated residuals by the current GARCH volatility forecast to generate tomorrow's distribution. This preserves real-tail structure while conditioning on today's volatility regime.

5. Where ML enters market risk

GARCH-ML hybrids: replacing the volatility equation

The GARCH(1,1) volatility equation uses only two lagged inputs. ML models — in particular LSTMs and gradient boosted trees — can take dozens of inputs: realised volatility measures, options-implied volatility (VIX), macro variables, cross-asset signals. This consistently outperforms GARCH on out-of-sample volatility forecasting in equity and FX markets. 

σ²_t  ← ML(σ²_{t-1}, ..., σ²_{t-p},
              realised_vol, VIX, macro_features, ...)

Neural network quantile regression

Rather than modelling the full distribution and deriving quantiles, train a neural network directly to predict the α-quantile of losses (the VaR) using a pinball loss function: 

L(q, y, ŷ) = q × (y − ŷ)    if y > ŷ  (underestimate)
             = (1 − q) × (ŷ − y)  if y ≤ ŷ  (overestimate)

Minimising pinball loss at q = 0.99 → direct VaR estimate

Advantages: no distributional assumption; can model asymmetric tails; can include rich feature sets. Limitation: the output is a single quantile, not a full distribution — ES requires additional modelling of the region beyond the VaR threshold.

Deep learning for full distribution estimation

Normalising flows and conditional GANs can learn the full conditional return distribution given a feature set. These are research-stage in most banks but appear in advanced market risk groups at large dealer banks. The output is a synthetic P&L distribution that can be used for both VaR and ES at any confidence level.

6. FRTB context: why ES replaced VaR

The FRTB (Fundamental Review of the Trading Book), finalised by Basel in 2019, replaced VaR with ES as the primary capital measure under the Internal Models Approach (IMA). Three reasons:

  • VaR is not sub-additive — it can suggest that the risk of a combined portfolio exceeds the sum of its parts, which is irrational. ES is sub-additive and therefore correctly rewards diversification.
  • VaR ignores the tail beyond the threshold — two portfolios with identical VaR can have wildly different ES (one has a moderate tail; one a catastrophic tail). ES discriminates between them.
  • 2008 demonstrated VaR's failure — the crisis produced losses far exceeding 99% VaR on consecutive days. Regulators concluded the measure was inadequate for capital purposes.
The FRTB numbers
FRTB IMA uses ES at 97.5% over a 10-day horizon (using a 12-month historical lookback for the stress period). This is broadly equivalent to VaR at 99%, but with the tail properly measured. Banks must also pass a P&L attribution test and backtesting requirements before IMA approval is granted — and must use stressed market data from a period of financial turmoil.

7. Backtesting VaR — and why it matters for governance

Backtesting compares realised daily P&L against model VaR estimates. A 99% VaR model should be breached on approximately 2–3 days per year (1% of 250 trading days). Too many breaches means the model underestimates risk; too few may mean it is overly conservative (and over-capitalised).

Exceptions in 250 daysBasel zoneImplication
0–4GreenModel acceptable; no capital multiplier
5–9YellowModel under scrutiny; capital multiplier applied (k = 3.4–3.85)
10+RedModel rejected for IMA; forced to standardised approach

For ML-based VaR models, validators must demonstrate that the backtesting record is sound not just in the training period but in out-of-sample and stress periods. A model that backtests well in benign conditions but produces excess exceptions in 2008-equivalent scenarios will not receive regulatory approval.

Retrieval Practice

1. A portfolio's 1-day 99% VaR is £1m. What does this mean precisely?

Correct. VaR is a threshold, not a prediction of the specific loss. There is a 1% probability that tomorrow's loss will exceed £1m — and no information about how much larger it might be if that 1% event occurs. That gap is what ES addresses.
VaR is a quantile, not an expected or maximum value. 99% VaR of £1m means: on 1% of trading days (≈2–3 per year), the loss will exceed £1m. The size of loss on those days is not captured by VaR — that is what ES measures.

2. What is the key advantage of Expected Shortfall (ES) over VaR for capital calculation?

Correct. ES = E[Loss | Loss > VaR]. It averages the losses in the tail, so two portfolios with identical VaR but different tail shapes (one moderate, one catastrophic) will have different ES. ES also satisfies sub-additivity — it always rewards diversification — which VaR does not.
ES captures tail severity. Two portfolios with identical VaR can have very different ES if their tails behave differently. ES also satisfies sub-additivity (a mathematical coherence property VaR lacks), meaning it always gives credit for diversification. These are the reasons FRTB replaced VaR with ES.

3. In GARCH(1,1), what does the ARCH term α measure?

Correct. α is the ARCH coefficient — it measures how much a large return shock (ε²_{t-1}) feeds into today's variance estimate. High α means volatility reacts sharply to news. β is the persistence term — how much of yesterday's variance estimate carries forward. Together α + β measures how long volatility shocks decay.
In GARCH(1,1): σ²_t = ω + α × ε²_{t-1} + β × σ²_{t-1}. α is the ARCH (reaction) term — it scales how much last period's squared return shock feeds into today's variance. β is the GARCH (persistence) term — it scales how much of yesterday's variance estimate carries over. α + β must be < 1 for the model to be stationary.

4. Under FRTB, what replaces 99% VaR as the primary capital measure?

Correct. FRTB IMA uses ES at 97.5% over a 10-day horizon. The 97.5% ES is broadly calibrated to be comparable with 99% VaR in terms of capital impact, but now properly measures the tail severity that VaR ignores.
FRTB uses ES at 97.5%, 10-day horizon. The confidence level is lower than the old VaR threshold (99%) because ES at 97.5% captures more tail mass than VaR at 99% — they are roughly equivalent in capital terms while ES is a more coherent and informative risk measure.

5. A 99% VaR model produces 12 backtesting exceptions in a 250-day window. What is the regulatory consequence under Basel?

Correct. 10+ exceptions in 250 days puts the model in the red zone. The regulator withdraws IMA approval and the bank must use the (typically more capital-intensive) Standardised Approach. This creates a strong incentive to maintain model quality and avoid model failure in stressed periods.
12 exceptions > 10 = red zone. IMA approval is revoked. The bank must use the Standardised Approach, which is typically more punitive on capital. This regulatory consequence makes VaR backtesting a critical governance metric — model failures in stressed periods are very expensive.
