Lesson 10 · Fraud & AML

Anti-Money Laundering with ML

How ML reduces the 90%+ false positive rate of rule-based AML — and why graph structure is the key signal rules cannot capture.

1. The rule-based AML crisis

Traditional AML systems are rule-based transaction monitoring: a set of manually defined thresholds and patterns that trigger an alert when hit. Classic rules include:

  • Cash transactions above £10,000
  • Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds (structuring / smurfing)
  • Wire transfers to high-risk jurisdictions
  • Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts ("layering")

These rules are necessary — but they generate an enormous volume of alerts. Industry data consistently puts the false positive rate at 90–99%: for every 100 alerts reviewed by a compliance analyst, 1–10 are genuine suspicions. The rest are legitimate transactions that happen to match a pattern. This creates:

  • Compliance team burnout and high staff turnover
  • Enormous operational costs (global banks spend £1–3bn annually on AML operations)
  • Alert fatigue leading to genuine SAR filings being missed
  • Regulatory risk: regulators penalise banks for poor AML operational effectiveness, not just for missing launderers
The regulatory context
AML is governed by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) internationally, with national implementation via the UK's Proceeds of Crime Act, the US Bank Secrecy Act, and EU's 6th Anti-Money Laundering Directive (6AMLD). Failure to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) on genuine money laundering carries criminal penalties for the institution and individual officers. The stakes are not just financial — they are existential for senior compliance executives.

2. Where ML improves AML

Alert triage

XGBoost / Random Forest

Scores incoming rule-based alerts by true-positive probability. Prioritises the alert queue — analysts investigate the highest-scoring alerts first. Reduces effective FP rate without changing the underlying rules.

Network detection

Graph ML / GNNs

Analyses the transaction network — accounts as nodes, transfers as edges — to identify suspicious structural patterns invisible at the individual transaction level.

NLP on communications

Transformers / BERT

Analyses SARs, customer communications, adverse media, and trade documents to extract risk signals and assist analysts with investigation.

3. Graph ML: where the signal lives in AML

Money laundering is inherently a network phenomenon. The classic three-stage process — placement (cash into the financial system), layering (obscuring the trail through multiple transfers), and integration (reinserting clean money) — produces distinctive graph structures that individual transaction rules cannot detect.

Two classic money laundering network patterns. Individual transactions look legitimate; the structure reveals the scheme.

Graph feature engineering (classical approach)

Before GNNs, graph-based AML used manually engineered network features fed into gradient boosted models:

  • In-degree / out-degree: how many unique counterparties send to / receive from this account
  • Fan-in / fan-out ratio: accounts that receive from many but send to few (aggregators) or vice versa (distributors)
  • Cycle detection: does money return to its origin within N hops?
  • Temporal velocity: how fast do funds move through the account (layering leaves short dwell times)
  • Betweenness centrality: accounts sitting at critical chokepoints in the network — often structurally important for laundering

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

GNNs extend this by learning graph structure directly, without manual feature engineering. A GNN passes "messages" between neighbouring nodes — each account aggregates information from its transaction neighbours — over multiple rounds. After k rounds, each node's embedding captures the structure of its k-hop neighbourhood: 

h_v^(0) = account features (balance, age, product type, ...)
h_v^(k) = AGGREGATE( h_v^(k-1),  {h_u^(k-1) : u ∈ N(v)} )

Final embedding h_v^(K) → classification: suspicious / legitimate

GNNs are particularly powerful for AML because they can learn that an account is suspicious because of who it transacts with — even if the account's own transaction history looks benign. This is the core insight that rules cannot encode.

4. NLP in AML

Natural language processing appears in AML investigations in three main ways:

ApplicationModel typePurpose
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) analysis BERT / RoBERTa fine-tuned on SAR corpus Extract named entities (people, companies, amounts), classify typology, assist analysts drafting new SARs by retrieving similar historical cases
Adverse media screening NER + sentiment + topic classification Screen news and web content for mentions of customers in the context of financial crime, sanctions, or corruption
Trade finance document analysis Document AI / LayoutLM Detect inconsistencies in trade finance documents (invoice value vs. customs declaration; commodity description mismatches) — a key vector for trade-based money laundering (TBML)

5. False positive reduction: the operational metric

Unlike fraud detection where recall dominates, AML has a more balanced cost structure:

  • False negatives (missed laundering): criminal and regulatory consequence — very high cost
  • False positives (unnecessary alerts): analyst time, operational cost, and relationship damage with legitimate customers who are repeatedly de-risked or have transactions delayed — also high at scale

The typical ML deployment in AML therefore targets false positive reduction at constant recall: 

Objective: maintain detection rate ≥ X% (regulatory floor)
           while minimising false positives per analyst-hour

XGBoost and Random Forest applied to alert scoring (classifying existing rule-based alerts as likely-genuine vs. likely-false-positive) consistently achieve 30–60% reduction in false positives while holding recall at or above the pre-ML baseline. This is the most commercially deployed ML use case in AML today.

6. Model risk in AML: the defensive model problem

AML models are defensive models — their purpose is to detect what should not happen, not to optimise a financial outcome. This creates model risk governance challenges distinct from credit or market risk models:

ChallengeWhy it's hardGovernance response
Ground truth is unknown A SAR is filed on suspicion; whether the customer is actually laundering is rarely confirmed. Models trained on SARs are trained on suspected, not confirmed, cases. Use law enforcement feedback loops where available. Accept that precision cannot be measured directly. Focus on relative performance (ML vs rules) and recall proxies.
Selective labelling bias Only alerts that were investigated have labels. Transactions that never triggered a rule were never investigated — some may be money laundering that the model will also miss. Use semi-supervised or unsupervised components (anomaly detection) to surface cases outside the rule-triggered population.
Explainability to compliance teams Compliance analysts need to understand and document why an alert was generated — a black-box GNN score is insufficient for SAR narrative drafting. SHAP explanations at the alert level. GNN attention scores to highlight which accounts and transactions drove the suspicious classification.
Regulatory model risk expectations AML models face both financial regulation (model risk) and AML/CFT regulation. A compliance model deficiency can be an AML finding as well as an MRM finding. Full SR 11-7 lifecycle applies. Additionally, regulatory guidance (FinCEN Innovation Hour, FCA TechSprints) specifically addresses AI/ML in AML.
Retrieval Practice

1. Rule-based AML systems generate false positive rates of 90–99%. What is the primary operational consequence?

Correct. When 95+ out of 100 alerts are false positives, analysts spend nearly all their time on legitimate customers. Alert fatigue sets in — the genuine suspicion buried in alert #87 of the day gets the same cursory review as the first 86. This is both an operational risk and a regulatory risk: regulators penalise banks for inadequate AML operations, not just for missing specific cases.
The operational consequence is alert fatigue and burnout. When analysts see 95%+ false positives, they lose the discrimination signal — every alert starts to look the same. The genuine SAR buried in a queue of 2,000 false alarms gets insufficient investigation time. Regulators have cited poor alert prioritisation (not just missed cases) as an AML control deficiency at multiple large banks.

2. In a GNN for AML, what does k-hop message passing capture that individual transaction rules cannot?

Correct. Message passing aggregates neighbourhood information — after k rounds, a node's embedding reflects the structure of its k-hop subgraph. An account can be flagged as suspicious because its 2nd-degree counterparties (counterparties of counterparties) are known bad actors, even if the account's own transactions look legitimate. Rules operate on individual transactions; GNNs operate on the network structure.
k-hop message passing lets the GNN look beyond the immediate transaction to the broader network. After k rounds of aggregation, each node embedding captures whether there are suspicious entities nearby in the graph — at 1 hop (direct counterparties), 2 hops (counterparties of counterparties), etc. A mule account that receives from a clean-looking source which itself received from a fraud ring is caught at 2 hops. Rules only see the direct transaction.

3. A BERT model is fine-tuned on historical SAR filings for AML. What is the "selective labelling bias" risk in training data?

Correct. Selective labelling: the training population is "transactions that generated a rule-based alert and were investigated." Transactions that are money laundering but never triggered a rule — entire typologies the rule set doesn't cover — are in neither the training positives nor negatives. The model cannot learn to detect what it has never been exposed to. This is a fundamental limitation of supervised AML models and a reason to complement them with unsupervised anomaly detection.
Only cases that triggered an alert were investigated; only investigated cases that seemed suspicious generated SARs. Money laundering that never hit a rule threshold — entire novel typologies — appears in neither the positive nor negative training labels. The model is trained on a censored version of reality. Unsupervised graph anomaly detection and autoencoder-based approaches partially address this by surfacing patterns the rule set never considered.

4. ML-based alert scoring in AML typically targets which operational objective?

Correct. The objective is false positive reduction at maintained recall — not recall maximisation. AML has a regulatory floor on detection rate that must be preserved. ML earns its value by reducing the analyst burden (FP reduction) without reducing the detection of genuine cases. 30–60% FP reduction is a consistently cited benchmark in deployed ML AML systems.
The objective is FP reduction at constant recall. Regulators set an implicit floor on detection — dropping recall to reduce FPs would be a compliance failure. ML adds value by using predictive scoring to deprioritise or dismiss the alerts most likely to be false positives, letting analysts spend their time on the 5–10% of alerts that matter. Recall is preserved; analyst efficiency multiplies.
