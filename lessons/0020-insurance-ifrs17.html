Insurance Models — IFRS17, Claims ML, and Longevity Risk

How ML integrates with actuarial science — and why the IFRS17 accounting standard changed what banks' insurance subsidiaries must model.

1. The insurance business model and its risk types

Insurance companies (and bank-owned insurance subsidiaries) collect premiums upfront and pay claims later — the opposite of lending. This creates a distinctive risk profile:

Risk typeDescriptionML application
Insurance riskActual claims exceed expected — frequency and severity worse than pricedClaims frequency/severity ML models; catastrophe models
Longevity riskAnnuity holders live longer than expected — more payments than priced (annuities and pensions)Lee-Carter + ML mortality forecasting; survival analysis
Mortality riskLife insurance policyholders die sooner — larger claims than expectedUnderwriting ML; mortality prediction models
Market riskInvestment portfolio losses reduce ability to pay future claimsAsset-Liability Management (ALM); duration matching ML
Catastrophe riskExtreme weather, pandemics, or man-made catastrophes produce correlated claimsCat models (RMS, AIR); climate SEG from Lesson 16

2. IFRS17 — the new insurance accounting standard

IFRS17 (effective January 2023) replaced IFRS4 with a comprehensive standard for insurance contract accounting. It requires insurance liabilities to be measured using the Building Block Approach (BBA):

IFRS17 Liability = Fulfilment Cash Flows + Contractual Service Margin (CSM)

Fulfilment Cash Flows:
  = Present Value of Future Cash Flows (PVFCF)
    + Risk Adjustment (RA)

Where:
  PVFCF = discounted expected future claims + expenses − future premiums
  RA    = compensation for non-financial risk bearing (typically set at 75th percentile)
  CSM   = unearned profit, released over coverage period as service is provided

Key modelling implications: all cash flow projections must use current market-consistent discount rates, expected cash flows must capture the full probability distribution of outcomes, and the Risk Adjustment requires a probability of sufficiency choice (typically 75th percentile — meaning the RA covers outcomes up to the 75th percentile of the outcome distribution). This requires stochastic claims models, not just expected value projections.

3. Claims modelling with ML

The core insurance ML application is improving claims prediction over the actuarial baseline. Claims are typically modelled in two stages:

  • Frequency model: predicts the number of claims per policy per year. Classical model: Poisson GLM. ML improvement: XGBoost or neural network capturing non-linear feature interactions (claims history × age × exposure location) that GLM misses.
  • Severity model: predicts the cost per claim. Classical model: Gamma GLM (right-skewed losses). ML improvement: quantile regression forests or gradient boosting on claims cost, with separate sub-models for large loss events.
Pure premium (expected cost) = Frequency × Severity
                              = E[claims/policy] × E[cost/claim]

ML goal: more accurate joint frequency-severity prediction
         → fairer pricing, better reserve estimates, competitive advantage

SHAP values on claims models help identify which features drive pricing — critical for regulatory scrutiny (UK FCA pricing fairness rules prohibit using certain proxy variables such as postcode as a proxy for ethnicity).

4. Reserving — the IBNR problem

Claims reserving estimates how much the insurer must hold for Incurred But Not Reported (IBNR) claims — losses that have happened but not yet been filed. The classical actuarial tool is the chain ladder method (development factors applied to triangle of paid claims). ML improvements:

  • Reserving neural networks (ResNets for triangles): train a neural network directly on development triangles. Can detect patterns the chain ladder misses — particularly for lines where development factors vary by accident year.
  • Stochastic chain ladder (bootstrapping): bootstrap the residuals of chain ladder fits to produce a distribution of reserve estimates — needed for IFRS17 Risk Adjustment and Solvency II SCR calculation.
  • Tail factor estimation: ML on industry-wide data improves tail development factor estimation when individual company data is too sparse.

5. Longevity risk — the actuarial ML frontier

For pension annuities and life insurance, longevity risk is the risk that policyholders live longer than projected. The classical mortality model is Lee-Carter:

ln(μ_xt) = α_x + β_x · κ_t + ε_xt

where: μ_xt  = mortality rate for age x at time t
       α_x   = age-specific baseline mortality
       β_x   = age-specific sensitivity to mortality trend
       κ_t   = time trend (improving mortality = falling κ_t)
       ε_xt  = noise term

Lee-Carter captures the historical trend of improving mortality but struggles with: (a) cohort effects (certain birth years show unexpectedly low/high mortality), (b) pandemic shocks (COVID 2020-21), and (c) structural breaks in mortality improvement trends. ML improvements:

  • Deep neural networks for mortality: DNNs on age-period-cohort data capture cohort effects automatically without the need to specify the cohort structure a priori
  • Gradient boosting on socioeconomic features: postcode-level income, deprivation index, smoking rates, and BMI data improve individual-level mortality prediction for pricing and portfolio management
  • Scenario generation: VAE/GAN-generated mortality improvement scenarios for IFRS17 stochastic cash flow projections

6. Catastrophe models

Catastrophe events (hurricanes, earthquakes, pandemics) produce correlated losses across the insurer's entire portfolio. Dedicated cat models (commercial platforms: RMS, AIR, Verisk) simulate thousands of catastrophe scenarios and estimate industry-wide and company-specific losses. ML role:

  • Improved windspeed/flood depth prediction from physical climate models → more accurate footprints
  • Computer vision (satellite/aerial imagery) for post-event damage assessment — replacing manual inspection for large-scale events
  • NLP on claims reports for early severity estimation after a catastrophe event
IFRS17 and model risk governance
IFRS17 requires that all assumptions (discount rates, claims development, mortality improvement, risk adjustment methodology) be documented, justified, and audited. The external auditor reviews the actuarial model adequacy. For insurers with ML-driven cash flow models, this creates a new model risk governance requirement: models must be interpretable and their assumptions documented with sufficient clarity for audit sign-off. SHAP-based explainability is rapidly becoming standard practice for claims and reserving ML models in audit contexts.
Retrieval Practice

1. Under IFRS17 BBA, what does the Contractual Service Margin (CSM) represent, and how is it recognised in P&L?

Correct. The CSM prevents immediate recognition of "day one profit" — the present value of premiums exceeds the present value of expected claims for a profitable contract. This unearned profit sits in the CSM balance sheet liability and is released into insurance service revenue over the period during which the insurer provides coverage. This matches revenue recognition to service delivery — a fundamental principle of IFRS15/17.
The CSM is the IFRS17 mechanism for matching profit recognition to service delivery. On day one, if a contract is profitable (PV premiums > PV claims + expenses), that surplus is locked in the CSM rather than recognised immediately. It is then released systematically over the coverage period as the insurer provides insurance service — aligning revenue with the period during which risk is borne. Unprofitable contracts produce a loss component instead, which IS recognised immediately.

2. Why is the two-stage claims model (frequency × severity) better structured than a single model predicting total claims cost directly?

Correct. Claims frequency (Poisson-distributed count) and severity (Gamma or log-normal distributed cost) have fundamentally different statistical properties and different predictor relationships. A young male driver has high frequency but low average severity; a commercial fleet may have low frequency but catastrophic individual losses. Separating the models allows each component to be calibrated on its own data generating process, with separate feature sets and distributions. It also allows pricing stress-tests on each component independently.
Claim count and claim cost have different distributions (Poisson vs Gamma/lognormal) and respond differently to risk factors. Driver age strongly predicts claim count; vehicle value strongly predicts cost per claim. A single model conflating these creates specification error. The frequency × severity structure also enables separate analysis: why did my book experience increase? Was it more claims (frequency up) or larger claims (severity up)? Each has a different management response.

3. What is the IBNR problem in insurance reserving, and what makes ML reserving approaches potentially superior to the chain ladder method?

Correct. IBNR claims create the reserving challenge: at year-end, the insurer knows premiums earned but not the total cost of claims for that year, since some haven't been filed yet. Chain ladder estimates development patterns from historical triangles but assumes stationary development factors — an assumption that breaks for new lines of business, volatile claim environments, or lines with long settlement tails. ML neural networks on triangles learn development patterns without the stationarity assumption and can capture accident-year-specific effects that chain ladder averages out.
IBNR is the gap between claims that have occurred and claims that have been reported and processed. Every year-end, the balance sheet must reserve for this. Chain ladder extrapolates historical development triangles but imposes a strong stationarity assumption (development factors are the same every accident year). ML improves on this by learning non-stationary patterns: a triangle showing accelerating development in recent years (perhaps due to litigation trends) would be captured by an ML model and missed by a classical chain ladder average.
