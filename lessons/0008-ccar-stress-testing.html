Lesson 08 · Market Risk

CCAR & Stress Testing with ML

How non-linear models make stress scenarios more granular — and why the regulator's challenge function makes interpretability non-negotiable.

1. What CCAR demands of banks

CCAR (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review) is the Federal Reserve's annual stress testing programme for large US bank holding companies. The UK equivalent is the Bank of England's Annual Cyclical Scenario (ACS). The European Banking Authority runs EU-wide stress tests on a similar cadence.

The core question: If the economy deteriorates severely, does the bank still have enough capital to keep lending? The Fed prescribes macroeconomic scenarios — banks must project their own losses, revenues, and capital ratios under those scenarios using their internal models.

Severely Adverse Scenario — stylised CCAR inputs
Unemployment+5.5pp → 10%
GDP growth−5% peak
Equity markets−55%
House prices−25%
Corp credit spread+375bp
Short-term ratesnear zero

Banks feed these macro variables into satellite models that translate them into portfolio-level losses: credit losses, market risk losses, pre-provision net revenue (PPNR), and operational risk. The final output is a projected Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio across a 9-quarter horizon — if it stays above the regulatory minimum, the bank passes.

The classical approach: regress historical loan loss rates (or PD/LGD changes) on lagged macro variables using OLS or panel regression. The fitted coefficients translate each macro variable's movement into an expected change in portfolio losses.

Example — a simple retail credit loss satellite model:

Loss_rate_t = α
             + β₁ × ΔUnemployment_t
             + β₂ × ΔGDP_t
             + β₃ × ΔHousePrice_t
             + ε_t

Plug in the scenario values → get a quarterly loss rate → multiply by EAD → get credit losses. Simple, transparent, and examinable by regulators. The limitation: macro-loss relationships are non-linear, regime-dependent, and differ across portfolio segments — exactly what OLS cannot capture.

3. Where ML improves on satellite models

Non-linear shock transmission

Unemployment rising from 4% to 5% has a very different impact on consumer credit losses than rising from 8% to 10% — the relationship is concave at low unemployment and convex as it approaches a crisis level. OLS forces linearity. ML (XGBoost, neural networks) learns the true non-linear shape from the data.

Interaction effects between macro variables

High unemployment plus falling house prices plus tight credit conditions produces losses far exceeding the sum of each factor's individual effect — a joint shock that linear models systematically underestimate. Gradient boosted trees capture these cross-variable interactions naturally.

Granular projection

Instead of a single portfolio-level satellite model, ML can produce account-level or segment-level loss projections. This enables the bank to identify which segments are most stressed under each scenario — a capability that is increasingly demanded by regulators who want to understand the distribution of losses, not just the aggregate.

ML-generated stress scenarios

An emerging application: use Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) or Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to generate adversarial stress scenarios — the worst plausible macro paths that are not in the historical dataset. The generator learns the joint distribution of macro variables and produces novel scenarios that respect economic constraints (e.g., unemployment cannot fall and credit spreads cannot tighten simultaneously with GDP collapsing).

Adversarial vs regulatory scenarios
ML-generated adversarial scenarios complement but do not replace regulator-prescribed scenarios. Banks use them for internal stress testing, reverse stress testing (find the scenario that causes failure), and climate stress testing (where historical data is scarce). The regulatory CCAR submission still uses Fed-prescribed scenarios — ML scenarios inform the internal view.

4. The out-of-sample problem: stress testing's hardest challenge

All ML models face a common problem in stress testing: the scenarios being tested are outside the training data by design. A severely adverse scenario is a tail event — it has never happened, or has happened rarely. This creates a fundamental epistemological problem for any model:

  • A model trained on 2000–2020 data will capture the 2008 crisis, the 2011 European sovereign crisis, and COVID. But a scenario with simultaneous 55% equity decline and 10% unemployment and falling rates — the "triple shock" — may have no exact historical precedent.
  • ML models are particularly susceptible: tree-based models extrapolate poorly beyond the range of training data. The prediction at unemployment = 14% is simply the prediction at the maximum unemployment seen in training, extended flat.
  • Neural networks can extrapolate more smoothly but may do so incorrectly if the functional form in the extreme tail differs from the training range.
The conservatism overlay
Banks routinely apply management overlays to ML stress test outputs: expert judgment adjustments that increase projected losses in areas where the model's extrapolation is uncertain. These overlays must themselves be documented, justified, and validated. A bank that uses an overlay every year without documenting its basis is creating a model risk finding ("undocumented model adjustment"). The overlay is not a free pass — it is itself model risk governed.

5. Interpretability under regulatory challenge

CCAR stress models face a uniquely intense interpretability requirement: the Federal Reserve's challenge function. Examiners review bank models and can:

  • Reject projections as implausible and substitute their own estimates
  • Issue Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs) or Matters Requiring Immediate Attention (MRIAs) for model deficiencies
  • Require full re-submission if the stress test is found materially non-compliant

For an ML stress model to survive regulatory challenge, it must:

  1. Produce directionally sensible responses — credit losses must rise with unemployment, fall with GDP growth. SHAP / PDP analysis must confirm this for examiners.
  2. Have stable sensitivity — a 1pp change in unemployment should produce a consistent, documented loss impact. Reviewers will run sensitivity tests.
  3. Be independently validated — the stress model validation team must have run challenger models (e.g., a linear satellite model as challenger to the ML model) and documented why the ML model is preferred.
  4. Have documented conservatism — where the model extrapolates into the tail, overlays must be justified in writing and approved by the Model Risk Committee.
SR 15-18: the stress test governance standard
The Fed's SR 15-18 letter provides detailed expectations for stress testing model risk management. It explicitly applies SR 11-7's full MRM framework to stress models — development, validation, governance, ongoing monitoring — and adds stress-test-specific requirements on scenario sensitivity and documentation. Reading this alongside SR 11-7 gives the full governance picture.

Retrieval Practice

1. What is the output of a CCAR stress test that determines whether a bank "passes"?

Correct. The stress test output is a projected capital ratio (CET1) over 9 quarters under the severely adverse scenario. If the bank's CET1 remains above the regulatory minimum (4.5% + buffers), it passes. Failing can trigger restrictions on dividends and share buybacks.
CCAR's final test is the capital ratio trajectory. Banks must show that even under the severely adverse scenario, their CET1 ratio stays above the regulatory minimum over a 9-quarter horizon. Credit losses, PPNR, and market losses all feed into this calculation, but the pass/fail criterion is the capital ratio floor.

2. Why do traditional OLS satellite models systematically underestimate losses in a severe stress scenario?

Correct. The relationship between, say, unemployment and credit losses is non-linear — the marginal impact of each additional percentage point of unemployment grows at extreme levels. OLS forces a constant slope, so it extrapolates linearly beyond the training range. ML captures the non-linearity from the data, producing higher (more conservative) loss estimates in severe tail scenarios.
OLS forces a linear, constant-slope relationship between macro inputs and losses. In reality, the loss response accelerates at extremes — a 10% unemployment rate causes disproportionately more losses than a linear model predicts relative to 5% unemployment. ML captures this non-linear shape. OLS extrapolates linearly into the tail, systematically understating losses exactly when the stress test is most important.

3. A bank applies a management overlay, increasing projected credit losses by 15% above the ML model output for the severely adverse scenario. What must the bank do to satisfy SR 11-7 / SR 15-18?

Correct. SR 15-18 (and SR 11-7 applied to stress models) requires that overlays be documented with a clear rationale, approved through the appropriate governance process, and reviewed during model validation. An undocumented or habitual overlay that is never revisited is itself a model risk finding — it becomes an unofficial model component without governance.
Management overlays are not exempt from governance. SR 15-18 requires the basis for any overlay to be documented (e.g., "model extrapolates beyond its training range for unemployment > 9%; overlay reflects expert judgment on tail non-linearity"), approved by the MRC, and reviewed at the next validation cycle. Routine, undocumented overlays are a top CCAR model risk finding.

4. Why are GANs and VAEs used for stress scenario generation rather than directly in CCAR regulatory submissions?

Correct. The Fed prescribes the specific macro paths for official CCAR submissions — banks cannot substitute their own scenarios. ML scenario generation is valuable for internal purposes: reverse stress testing (what scenario would cause us to fail?), climate scenarios (where there is no historical data), and internal board stress tests that go beyond the regulatory minimum.
The regulatory CCAR submission must use the Fed's prescribed scenarios. No substitution is permitted. ML-generated scenarios are used internally — for reverse stress testing (finding the failure scenario), deep tail exploration, climate risk (where historical data is limited), and internal management information. They inform risk decisions without appearing in the regulatory capital submission.
