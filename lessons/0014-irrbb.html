Lesson 14 · Banking Book Risk

IRRBB: Interest Rate Risk in the Banking Book

Why retail deposits and mortgage prepayments make interest rate risk uniquely hard to model — and how ML captures what classical models miss.

1. Banking book vs trading book

Market risk (Lessons 5–6) covers the trading book: instruments held for short-term profit, marked to market daily. IRRBB covers the banking book: loans, deposits, mortgages, and investment securities held on an accrual basis to maturity. The same interest rate move has very different effects:

Trading BookBanking Book
AccountingMark-to-market (fair value)Accrual / amortised cost (mostly)
Rate impactImmediate P&L via MTM changeGradual impact on net interest income over time; EVE change captures long-run value
Regulatory frameworkFRTB (traded book capital)BCBS 368 / IRRBB standard (Pillar 2 capital)
Key riskShort-term mark-to-market lossNet interest income compression; erosion of equity value

2. Two lenses: EVE and NII / BBES

EVE — Economic Value of Equity

What it measures: Present value of all future asset cash flows minus present value of all future liability cash flows. A rate shock changes EVE by changing the discount rates applied to these cash flows.

Horizon: Full remaining life of the balance sheet
Use case: Long-run solvency; used in Pillar 2 capital assessments

Rate rises → asset values fall more than liability values (for a typical bank with more long-term fixed-rate assets than liabilities) → EVE falls

NII / BBES — Net Interest Income / Banking Book Earnings Sensitivity

What it measures: Projected net interest income over a 1–3 year horizon under a rate shock scenario, compared to the base case.

Horizon: Short-to-medium term (1–3 years)
Use case: Earnings impact; used in budgeting and stress testing

Rate rises → short-term asset repricing benefit (floating-rate loans, maturing fixed-rate assets repriced higher) but also higher deposit costs as customers demand better rates

The two measures can conflict: a bank can be positioned to benefit from rising rates in NII (short-term earnings) while being negatively exposed on EVE (long-run value). Risk management requires balancing both simultaneously.

3. The six standard IRRBB rate shock scenarios

BCBS 368 mandates that banks compute EVE and NII sensitivity under six standard interest rate shock scenarios:

ScenarioDescriptionKey risk captured
Parallel upAll tenors shift up by a prescribed amount (e.g., +200bp)Repricing mismatch between assets and liabilities
Parallel downAll tenors shift down (e.g., −200bp, floored at zero)Impact on floating-rate income; optionality in deposits and mortgages
SteepenerShort rates fall, long rates riseDuration mismatch; long-term assets gain, short-term liabilities reprice down
FlattenerShort rates rise, long rates fallCarry compression; short-term funding costs rise, long-term asset yields fall
Short rate upOnly short-term rates riseDeposit repricing, overnight and short-term funding costs
Short rate downOnly short-term rates fallFloor effects; deposit floor optionality, prepayment incentive on mortgages

4. The core modelling challenge: non-maturity deposits

The most difficult and commercially significant modelling problem in IRRBB is Non-Maturity Deposits (NMDs) — current accounts, savings accounts, and instant-access deposits that have no contractual maturity. The customer can withdraw at any time.

Yet in practice, most of this money is behaviourally sticky — customers leave deposits in place even when rates move, because the hassle of switching outweighs the rate gain. This stickiness has two dimensions that must be modelled separately:

Deposit beta and volume sensitivity: how much of a rate change is passed to depositors (beta), and how much volume leaves when rates diverge from market rates (volume sensitivity).

Deposit beta (pass-through)

Deposit beta = fraction of a market rate change that the bank passes on to depositors. Beta = 0 means the bank pays the same deposit rate regardless of market rates. Beta = 1 means the bank fully passes through every rate change. In practice, retail deposit betas are 0.2–0.6 in rising rate environments (banks pass through 40–80% less than the rate change) and often near zero for the first 50–100bp of rate rises.

Volume sensitivity

When deposit rates lag market rates too much, customers switch to higher-paying alternatives. This volume sensitivity must be modelled as a function of the rate gap between the deposit rate and the market rate.

ML for deposit behaviour

Classical NMD models used linear regression to estimate beta from historical data. ML improves in three ways:

  • Non-linearity: deposit beta is not constant — it behaves differently near zero rates (floor effects), at different levels of rate change magnitude, and in rising vs falling environments. XGBoost captures regime-conditional beta naturally.
  • Customer segmentation: betas differ dramatically across customer segments (retail vs SME, current account vs savings, high-balance vs low-balance). ML models trained at the account level and aggregated produce more accurate portfolio betas.
  • Lead/lag dynamics: deposit repricing lags market rate changes by weeks or months. LSTM models capturing the temporal structure of repricing cycles outperform static regression on multi-rate-cycle datasets.

5. Mortgage prepayment models

Fixed-rate mortgages carry prepayment optionality: when market rates fall, borrowers can refinance to a lower rate — effectively redeeming the mortgage early. This is an option the borrower holds and the bank has written. It creates an extension/contraction risk for the bank's balance sheet.

  • Rates fall → prepayments accelerate → bank must reinvest proceeds at lower rates
  • Rates rise → prepayments slow → bank is locked into below-market fixed-rate assets for longer

ML prepayment models outperform the classical logistic-regression and PSA (Public Securities Association) convention approaches by capturing:

  • Rate incentive non-linearity: the rate incentive to refinance follows an S-curve (very low incentive has little effect; beyond a threshold, incentive drives rapid prepayments)
  • Burnout effect: mortgages that have survived multiple refinancing opportunities despite incentive are less likely to prepay — the "easy refinancers" have already gone
  • Borrower heterogeneity: age, loan-to-value, employment type, and credit score all affect prepayment propensity independently of rate incentive

6. Governance: BCBS 368 and the Pillar 2 framework

IRRBB is regulated under Pillar 2 (supervisory review), not Pillar 1 (minimum capital). Banks must hold capital against IRRBB risk under their ICAAP (Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process), but the amount is based on internal models rather than a standardised formula.

BCBS 368 (2016) and the EBA IRRBB Guidelines (2022) specify:

  • The six standard shock scenarios must be computed and disclosed in Pillar 3 reports
  • A supervisory outlier test: if EVE decline under any standard shock exceeds 15% of Tier 1 capital, the regulator is alerted and may require capital or de-risking
  • NMD models must be documented with assumptions on repricing maturity (the assumed maturity of behaviourally sticky deposits) and volume sensitivity
  • Model assumptions must be reviewed at least annually and challenged by the ALCO (Asset and Liability Committee) and model validation
The 2022–2023 rate cycle as a stress test
The rapid rate rises of 2022–2023 provided the first real-world stress test of IRRBB models built during the low-rate era. Several US regional banks (notably SVB) had extreme EVE sensitivity to rate rises — their long-duration bond portfolios lost substantial value as rates rose, while their deposit models assumed much higher stickiness than materialised. This episode drove regulatory attention back to IRRBB model quality, particularly NMD assumptions and the interaction between EVE sensitivity and deposit flight under stress.
Retrieval Practice

1. A bank has predominantly long-term fixed-rate mortgages funded by short-term deposits. Interest rates rise sharply. What happens to EVE?

Correct. EVE = PV(assets) − PV(liabilities). Long-term fixed-rate mortgages have high duration — their PV falls significantly when discount rates rise. Short-term deposits have low duration — their PV barely changes. The asset PV falls more than the liability PV → EVE falls. This is the classic asset-liability mismatch. SVB's collapse in 2023 was a real-world example of this dynamic at extreme scale.
EVE measures the present value of assets minus liabilities. Long-term fixed-rate mortgages have high duration — a 200bp rate rise reduces their PV substantially. Short-term deposits have near-zero duration — their PV barely moves. Net result: asset PV falls much more than liability PV → EVE declines sharply. This is the textbook IRRBB asset-liability mismatch, and the reason SVB failed in 2023.

2. Deposit beta = 0.35 in a rising rate environment. Rates rise by 200bp. How much do deposit rates increase?

Correct. Deposit beta = fraction of rate change passed to depositors. Beta 0.35 × 200bp = 70bp passed on to customers. The bank retains the remaining 130bp as an interest margin boost — which is why rising rates are initially positive for NII (earnings) even while EVE falls. The tension between EVE and NII sensitivity is the central challenge ALCO must manage.
Beta = pass-through rate. 0.35 × 200bp = 70bp passed to depositors. The bank retains 200 − 70 = 130bp of the rate rise as margin, which improves NII. But this beta is not constant — competitors may attract deposit outflows if the gap between market rates and deposit rates becomes too wide, eventually forcing higher pass-through. Volume sensitivity captures this second-round effect.

3. The BCBS 368 supervisory outlier test flags a bank when EVE decline under a standard shock exceeds what threshold?

Correct. The BCBS 368 outlier threshold is 15% of Tier 1 capital. Breaching this in any of the six standard scenarios triggers regulatory attention — the supervisor may require additional Pillar 2 capital, balance sheet de-risking, or a remediation plan. It is not an automatic capital add-on but a supervisory escalation trigger.
BCBS 368 outlier test: if EVE decline > 15% of Tier 1 capital under any standard shock, the bank is flagged for supervisory review. The regulator may then require capital add-ons or impose de-risking requirements. The threshold is expressed as a fraction of Tier 1 (not RWA or total assets) to reflect that IRRBB risk is primarily an equity risk.

4. What is the "burnout effect" in mortgage prepayment modelling — and why does ML capture it better than classical models?

Correct. Burnout is a selection effect: when rates fell in prior periods and some borrowers refinanced, those remaining are disproportionately the ones who did not refinance despite having incentive — because of hassle, poor credit, or inertia. This pool is less sensitive to future refinancing incentives. Linear regression on current rate incentive cannot capture this history-dependent dynamic; ML models with lagged features (how many refinancing opportunities were missed) model it naturally.
Burnout is a path-dependent effect. A mortgage pool that passed through previous low-rate refinancing windows with some proportion refinancing leaves the remaining pool dominated by borrowers who were less rate-sensitive (due to credit constraints, inertia, or preference). This seasoned pool responds less to future rate incentives. Linear prepayment models using only the current rate incentive miss this — ML models with vintage and rate-history features capture it.
