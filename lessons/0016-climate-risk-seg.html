Lesson 16 · Climate & ESG Risk

Climate Risk & Scenario Generation

How physical and transition risks transmit into credit, market, and liquidity losses — and how ML extends scenario frameworks into climate territory with little historical data.

1. Two types of climate risk

Physical Risk

Losses from climate-related physical events: acute (floods, hurricanes, wildfires) and chronic (sea-level rise, heat stress, changing rainfall patterns).

Financial channel: collateral value destruction (flooded properties), supply chain disruption (corporate borrower revenues), stranded assets

Examples: A mortgage portfolio on UK flood-plain properties; a corporate loan to a coastal manufacturer

Transition Risk

Losses from the economic adjustment to a low-carbon economy: policy changes (carbon taxes, emissions trading), technology shifts (EV replacing ICE vehicles), reputational changes.

Financial channel: stranded assets (fossil fuel reserves), sector repricing (oil & gas, steel, aviation), competitive disruption

Examples: Loans to coal miners, car manufacturers, high-carbon real estate
TCFD and regulatory requirements
The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) framework requires banks to disclose climate risks across physical and transition dimensions. The PRA's SS3/19, the ECB's supervisory expectations, and the Bank of England's Climate Biennial Exploratory Scenario (CBES) have made climate stress testing mandatory for major UK and European banks.

2. NGFS climate scenarios

The Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS) provides the standard scenario framework used by central banks and supervisors globally. Three broad families:

Scenario familyTransition speedPhysical riskKey financial impact
Orderly (Net Zero 2050)Early, smooth transitionLow — warming contained to ~1.5°CModerate transition costs; low physical damage; manageable for banks
Disorderly (Delayed Transition)Late, abrupt transitionModerate — 1.8–2°C warmingHigh sudden transition costs; carbon prices spike; stranded assets across sectors
Hot House World (Current Policies)Minimal transitionVery high — 3°C+ warming by 2100Severe physical damages; agricultural disruption; GDP losses in tropical/coastal economies

3. How climate risk transmits into financial risk

Climate is not a standalone risk type — it amplifies existing financial risks through transmission channels:

  • Credit risk: physical damage destroys collateral value (flooded mortgages); transition disrupts borrower revenue (carbon-intensive sector loans → higher PD). The IFRS9 ECL model must incorporate climate-adjusted PD and LGD.
  • Market risk: carbon pricing and transition policy changes repricing equity and fixed income portfolios. Oil & gas equities fall; green infrastructure rises. Sudden policy announcements create VaR-event-scale volatility.
  • Liquidity risk: climate events can trigger sudden deposit outflows (affected communities) and funding market stress (if climate concerns trigger bank-run dynamics for high-carbon lenders).
  • Operational risk: physical damage to bank infrastructure (data centres, branch networks) from acute climate events.

4. Scenario Expansion and Generation (SEG) with ML

Climate scenario modelling faces a data scarcity problem: the climate scenarios we most need to stress-test (severe physical damage, abrupt transition) have no historical precedent. This is where SEG techniques — using ML to expand and generate scenarios — are most valuable.

Scenario expansion

Regulatory scenarios (NGFS) provide macro-level outputs: GDP, unemployment, carbon prices, sector revenues. Banks need these translated into thousands of granular risk factor paths — individual credit spreads, property values by postcode, sector-level PDs. ML (particularly GANs and VAEs trained on historical macro-financial relationships) expands the sparse scenario set into a rich distribution of plausible paths consistent with the macro narrative.

Physical risk ML: geospatial models

Physical risk assessment requires mapping individual exposures (properties, infrastructure) to climate hazard data at a granular level:

Physical risk score(property) = f(flood_zone, elevation, coastal_proximity,
                                   historical_damage_events, future_hazard_projection,
                                   adaptation_measures, building_vintage)

ML approach: Random Forest / XGBoost on geospatial features → property-level
             climate vulnerability score → LGD adjustment for mortgages

Transition risk: sector carbon intensity models

For corporate loan portfolios, transition risk is modelled by estimating each borrower's exposure to carbon pricing:

  • Map borrowers to NACE/SIC sectors and estimate Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions intensity
  • Apply carbon price paths from the NGFS scenario to estimate added cost burden
  • Translate cost burden → EBITDA impact → PD uplift under each scenario
  • ML improves the EBITDA-to-PD transmission: non-linear impacts at high carbon cost levels, sector-specific adjustment curves learned from historical carbon price episodes

5. Climate-adjusted ECL and IFRS9 overlay

Most banks currently apply climate risk to IFRS9 as a management overlay on top of their standard ECL models — largely because climate-adjusted PD models are still being developed. The overlay approach:

  1. Identify climate-sensitive segments of the portfolio (high flood-risk mortgages, fossil fuel sector loans)
  2. Estimate the climate-scenario ECL uplift for each segment (from physical/transition analysis)
  3. Apply as an overlay to the base ECL, documented and approved via MRC
  4. Disclose the overlay quantum in TCFD reporting and auditor-reviewed climate disclosures
Model risk in climate models
Climate models carry model risk of a distinctive type: uncertainty spans centuries and involves physical science, economics, and policy — all highly uncertain. Validators cannot back-test climate models against realised outcomes. The standard response is scenario-based uncertainty quantification (show results under multiple NGFS scenarios) and explicit documentation of key assumptions. The FRC and PRA are increasingly scrutinising the basis for climate overlay amounts.
Retrieval Practice

1. A bank has a large mortgage book on UK flood-plain properties. Which type of climate risk primarily affects these mortgages?

Correct. Flood-plain mortgages are primarily exposed to physical climate risk: acute flood events destroy collateral value and may render properties unmortgageable, while chronic sea-level rise causes long-term property price declines. Transition risk (carbon taxes, policy changes) has minimal direct effect on residential property collateral.
Physical risk affects collateral directly. Flood events reduce property values, increase repair costs, and potentially make properties uninsurable (insurers are already withdrawing from high-flood-risk areas). Lower collateral value → higher LGD on defaulted mortgages. Transition risk primarily affects borrowers in carbon-intensive industries — less relevant for residential mortgages.

2. In the NGFS "Disorderly" scenario, why are transition costs higher than in the "Orderly" scenario?

Correct. The key driver of transition cost magnitude is the speed and predictability of policy change. Early, gradual, predictable transition (Orderly) allows businesses and banks to adjust progressively. Late, sudden transition (Disorderly) means carbon prices must spike sharply to achieve the same emission reductions in less time — creating larger, faster asset repricing and stranded asset creation.
The Disorderly scenario's high transition costs arise from speed, not magnitude of eventual change. Both scenarios reach net zero or similar targets, but Disorderly does so abruptly after delayed action. The sudden carbon price jump and rapid policy change leave less time for business model adjustment, producing larger stranded assets and more disruptive sector repricing than a gradual Orderly transition.

3. Why does climate risk SEG (Scenario Expansion and Generation) rely on ML rather than purely using NGFS scenarios directly?

Correct. NGFS scenarios describe macro pathways: GDP growth, unemployment, carbon prices, temperature. A bank's credit portfolio needs risk factor granularity: flood risk by postcode, PD uplift by NACE sector, property price by region and vintage. ML learns the macro-to-granular relationships from historical data and uses them to expand the handful of NGFS scenarios into the thousands of granular paths needed for a full ECL calculation.
NGFS scenarios are public but sparse — a few macroeconomic variables across three scenario families. Loan-level climate risk assessment requires granular data: property-level flood depth, sector-level carbon cost burden, regional property price paths. ML bridges the gap by learning historical macro-to-granular relationships and extrapolating them into climate scenario space, producing the portfolio-level inputs that NGFS scenarios alone cannot provide.
